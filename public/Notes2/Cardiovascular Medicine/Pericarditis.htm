Pericardial Disease

-        Pericardium = fibrous layer covering the heart

-        2 layers: the inner visceral layer folds back on itself to form the outer parietal layer, thus creating a sac in between.

-        Normally contains ~50ml fluid

-        Lubricates heart surface

 

 

Acute Pericarditis

 

-        Inflammation of pericardium

-        Fibrous deposition ± pericardial effusion

-        presents similar to angina

-        Numerous causes but most commonly viral infection, post-MI or malignant (primary, breast mets, lung mets, lymphoma and leukaemia)

-        Also uraemia, autoimmune, postsurgical, familial

 

Ø  Viral

·        usu painful; short duration; no long term effects; most commonly cocksackie b, echovirus and HIV.

 

Ø  Post MI

·        occurs in ~5-20% in first few days following MI

·        mainly when full-thickness infarct (Q-waves?)

·        Less common since introduction of reperfusion therapy

·        Can also occur at a later stage (Dressler’s syndrome)

·        Need to exclude further ischaemic event – is chest pain due to re-infarction?

 

Clinical Features

 

-        Sharp central chest pain

-        Exacerbated by movement, respiration and lying down

-        Relieved by sitting forward

-        May radiate to neck or shoulders

-        Pericardial friction rub – best heard at LL sternal edge with patient sitting forward in held expiration

-        Fever, ↑WCC

 

Ø  Main DD are angina and pleurisy!

 

 

Distinguishing pericarditis from other causes of chest pain:

 

-        Pericarditis pain is

·        sharp (angina is dull)

·        radiates to left shoulder tip (angina radiates to arm)

·        worse on inspiration (similar to pleuritic pain)

·        worse on lying flat

·        often associated with a fever

 

 

 

Investigations

 

-        ECG is diagnostic

·        widespread ST elevation (note: in STEMI, the ST elevation is localised to specific leads)

·        concave upwards ‘saddle-shaped’ ST elevation

·        ECG evolves with time – ST changes, then T wave inverts/flattens, then returns to normal

 

-        Raised Troponin = myocarditis

 

Management

 

-        Bed rest and NSAIDs

·        However avoid NSAIDs in few days post-MI – increase risk of cardiac rupture

-        Treat underlying cause if present

-        Corticosteroids if does not resolve

 

-        ~20% develop relapsing idiopathic pericarditis

-        If lasts >6 months = chronic pericarditis and can go onto constrictive pericarditis.

 

 

 

Pericardial Effusion and Cardiac Tamponade

 

-        Fluid in pericardial space = pericardial effusion

-        If fluid impairs ventricular filling and embarrasses circulation = cardiac tamponade

-        Effusion often accompanies acute pericarditis (see causes above)

-        Cardiac tamponade can be a complication of dissected aortic aneurysm

 

Clinical features:

 

-        Soft heart sounds

-        Dyspnoea

-        Friction rub if pericarditis

-        Cardiac tamponade

·        ↑JVP

·        Pulsus paradoxus

·        ↓CO ie hypotension, fatigue, tachycardia

 

Causes of Pericardial effusion

 

-        Acute pericarditis (virus; post-MI)

-        Malignancy – pericardial mets from lung/breast

-        TB

-        Renal failure/uraemia

-        Hypothyroidism

-        RS/SLE/Systemic sclerosis

-        Trauma

 

Investigations

 

·        ECG – low voltage QRS complexes

·        CXR – shows large pear-shaped heart

·        Echo – best technique for demonstrating effusions

·        Pericardiocentesis – removal of fluid under US guidance; done if TB, malignant, or purulent effusion. Risky procedure.

 

Also: Blood cultures and autoantibody screens to look for underlying causes

 

Management

 

-        Most effusions resolve spontaneously

-        If tamponade – pericardiocentesis!

 

 

 

Constrictive Pericarditis

(VERY RARE)

 

-        Pericardium becomes thick, fibrous and calcified

-        To the extent that diastolic filling of the heart is impaired

-        Chronic condition

-        Causes: TB, haemopericardium, bacterial infection, rheumatic heart disease

 

Clinical Features

 

·        Systemic venous congestion – ascites, oedema, JVP, hepatosplenomegaly

·        Pulmonary congestion – dyspnoea, cough, orthopnoea, PND

·        Reduced cardiac output – fatigue, hypotension, tachycardia

·        AF – due to atrial dilatation

 

Investigations

 

-        CXR – small heart, pericardial calcification

-        ECG – low voltage QRS and T wave flattening/inversion

-        CT/MRI – assess pericardial thickness

 

DD – restrictive cardiomyopathy – exclude by pericardial biopsy

 

Management

 

-        Complete resection of pericardium

-        Risky operation
