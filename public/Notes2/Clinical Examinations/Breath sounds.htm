Breath Sounds

When listening to a normal chest…

 

“There was good air entry throughout the entire lung fields bilaterally. Breath sounds were vesicular in nature and there were no added sounds, such as crepitations or wheeze”

 

  • Vesicular breath sounds = soft, blowing sounds, inspiration is longer than expiration.

 

Abnormal breath sounds

 

  • Bronchial breathing – harsh, hollow quality; gap between inspiration and expiration. Heard when lung tissue has become solid eg consolidation; above a pleural effusion. May also be associated with increased vocal resonance as well as tactile vocal fremitus and whispering pectoriloquy (increased loudness of whisper over consolidation)

(Note: vocal resonance is lost over a pleural effusion!)

 

You are unlikely to get bronchial breathing in an exam – these patients tend to be acutely unwell!

 

  • Diminished breath sounds – occur if poor effort by patient, pleural effusion, pneumothorax, COPD.

 

  • Wheeze – is an expiratory sound created by air being expired through narrowed airways (eg asthma; COPD (chronic bronchitis)). Can be monophonic – single note due to partial obstruction of one airway (eg a tumour). Polyphonic – musical in nature; suggests widespread narrowing of airways of different calibre (eg asthma, COPD). Also hear wheeze in LVF - ‘cardiac asthma’.

 

  • Crackles/crepitations – caused by re-opening of airways that were occluded during expiration. Fine and high pitched if distal airways eg pulmonary fibrosis. Coarse and low pitched crackles if larger airways affected (eg pneumonia; bronchiectasis). Early inspiratory crackles suggest small airways disease, whereas late/pan-inspiratory crackles imply disease is confined to the alveoli. During inspiration the small airways open before the alveoli, so it makes sense that the more proximal the pathology the earlier the crackles are heard.

 

  • Pleural rub – low pitched grating or creaking noise described as being similar to the ‘sound made by walking on fresh snow’ eg a soft crunch. Occurs in pneumonia when inflammation is irritating the pleura. Present on inspiration and expiration. Can distinguish a pleural from a pericardial rub by asking the patient to hold their breath. If rub continues = pericardial rub eg pericarditis! Pericardial rub would be faster too!

 

OSCE tip: Remember to end a respiratory examination by saying you would want to measure Peak flow, check observations (esp RR, Temp, O2 sats), look in the sputum pot and see the patient’s Chest X-ray!
