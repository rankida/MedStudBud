Acne Vulgaris

-        Disorder of the pilosebaceous follicles

-        Due to hormones, grease and infection.

-        Affects face and upper trunk.

-        Most teenagers to some degree

·        More common in boys.

-        At puberty, androgens promote hypertrophy of sebaceous sweat glands. These secrete thick sebum, which can cause the glands to become blocked. Subsequent infection with the bacterium Propionibacterium acnes (P. acnes) then triggers an inflammatory reaction, resulting in acne.

 

Signs:

 

When describing acne (eg in an exam) the hallmark features to look out for are:

·        Comedones

·        Papules – small raised lesions

·        Pustules – papules filled with pus

·        Nodules – larger raised lesions

·        ± ice-pick scarring

 

-        Comedones

·        Follicles impacted with sebum and dead skin cells

·        Can be closed (blackheads) or open (whiteheads).

·        These are a key feature in the diagnosis of acne vulgaris.

·        When these become inflamed à papules, pustules and nodules.

 

-        Nodulocystic acne: severe acne with cysts. Very painful. Cysts can occur on face, neck or trunk and may lead to characteristic acne ‘ice-pick scarring’.

 

Differential diagnosis

 

Acne tends to be easy to recognise, especially given the typical population it presents in. Other differentials include:

 

-        Acne rosacea

·        the main differential for acne, esp in older adults

·        usually presents later. No comedones. butterfly distribution on face.

 

-        Milia: small keratin cysts. Look similar to whiteheads. Most common around the eyes.

 

 

Investigations

 

-        Usually no investigations are needed.

-        If new presentation in an atypical patient can refer to dermatology for investigations eg to exclude virilising tumour in a young yomen with acne and hirsutism.

 

Management

 

-        Empathetic advice and support

-        Wash face with soap and water twice daily (not more)

 

First line treatment of mild acne

 

-        Oxytetracycline + topical benzoyl peroxide

-        This is the most cost-effective combination

-        However it is poorly tolerated

·        Oxytetracycline needs to be taken on an empty stomach 30 mins before food (not actually so easy for a growing teenage boy wanting breakfast before school!)

·        It is also an inducer à decreases efficacy of OCP. This is not so good for teenage girls

·        And if a girl does fall pregnant on it, tetracyclines stain growing teeth and bones so need very effective contraception (implant).

·        Cannot be used on children <11 also because stains teeth

·        May cause photosensitivity

 

If not tolerated, swtich to Erythromycin.

 

For more severe acne

 

-        Refer to dermatologist à ?Roaccutaine (can only be prescribed by a specialist)

 

 

 

Topical preparations

 

-        Apply to all affected areas and not just existing lesions.

-        Can be difficult to apply to back.

 

Ø  Benzoyl peroxide:

 

-        Inhibits growth of P. acnes

-        Mildly irritant so can cause a burning sensation on application

-        Can also lead to peeling after a few days

-        Different strengths available 5%, 10%

-        Can be combined in preparations with topical antibiotics

 

Ø  Topical antibiotics:

 

-        Topical erythromycin and clindamycin.

-        Antibiotic resistance can be reduced by combining with zinc or benzoyl peroxide.

 

Ø  Topical Retinoids

 

-        most effective but v irritant:

-        eg adapalene (differin), isotretinoin or tretinoin

-        reduces comedones and has an anti-inflammatory effect.

-        Mild photosensitivity

-        Contra-indicated in pregnancy (even though systemic absorption is minimal).

 

 

Systemic treatments

 

-        Take months to work

-        Inhibit growth of P. acnes

 

Ø  Antibiotics:

 

-        Oxytetracycline 

·        First line – very cheap! BUT needs to be taken strictly 30 mins before food with water only so poor compliance.

 

-        Tetracyclines

·        Also has anti-inflammatory properties.

·        Lymocycline (aka tetralysal 300) less food dependent.

·        Vibromycin is an alternative.

·        NB: Tetracycline can stain growing teeth and bones – avoid in children and young women

 

-        Erythromycin or clindamycin

·        Use for children or women

 

-        Minocycline  

·        More effective but more s/e and more expensive

·        s/e: autoimmune hepatitis and SLE-like syndrome and so liver function tests and autoantibodies should be checked 3 months after starting treatment.

 

Ø  Anti-androgen treatment:

 

·        Eg Dianette – licensed for acne but effective as a contraceptive.

·        Yasmin is more expensive but not more effective.

 

Ø  Oral isotretinoin (Roaccutaine):

 

·        Very effective!

·        Reduces sebum secretion.

·        Can only be prescribed by hospital specialist.

·        S/e: Dry skin, lips and eyes. Raised serum lipids. Muscle aches and pains on strenuous exercise, hair thinning and acne flare-up also occur.

·        Need to confirm normal LFTs, U+Es and lipids prior to therapy.

·        TERATOGEN  - female patients must be on contraception, attend monthly pregnancy tests.

·  ?psychiatric side-effects – no evidence for causal relationship, however high profile in media so patients ask about.

 

Treatment for scarring:

 

·        Various Laser techniques, punch excisions and collagen injections can be used for severe acne scarring however it is important to warn patients that results are often disappointing

 

 

 

Complications

 

-        Psychological and social morbidity

-        Permanent scarring – ice-pick scars

 

Prognosis

-        Most cases clear up spontaneously by age 20 but some continue into adulthood.

 

 

Infantile acne

-        Acne can occur in infants and neonates, although it is rare

-        Most commonly affects cheeks

-        Usu resolves by 18 months

-        Topical antiseptics and antibiotics are first line treatment

-        If not responding seek specialist help early
