Gentamicin and Vancomycin

 

-        Important antibiotics

-        Increasingly used due to new prescribing protocols, which have removed antibiotics more prone to causing C. difficile infection and replaced them with Vanc and Gent

-        However require careful dose calculation (based on weight and creatinine clearance)

-        Also need regular drug monitoring

 

The 4Cs of C. difficile

 

The antibiotics most prone to leading to C diff are

 

= Cephalosporins, Co-amoxyclav, Clindamycin, Ciprofloxacin (all quinolones).

 

 

-        Be careful - mistakes in calculating the dose can lead to serious consequences

·        Treatment failure

·        Toxicity

 

This document is only a guide to help you understand the rationale behind these very important (and potentially dangerous) antibiotics. Follow local hospital policies when actually prescribing any antibiotic.

 

Gentamicin

 

-        Effective against Gram negative bacteria eg E. coli

-        Renally excreted, therefore elimination depends on renal function

-        Side-effects:

·        Nephrotoxic – damages kidneys

·        Ototoxic – damages hearing and balance (esp if prolonged use >10 days).

§  s/s:  tinnitus, dizziness, hearing loss, nausea

§  If >3 days – speak to Mirco. Warn pt about signs of ototoxicity

§  If >7days – need formal audiology assessment

 

General Principles:

 

1.     Does this patient need IV antibiotics?

2.     Calculate Initial dose

·        To do this log onto NHS staffnet and use the online calculator

3.     Measure concentration at 6-14 hours after the START of the infusion

4.     Adjust dose based on the normogram graph

5.     Monitor U+Es

6.     Does this patient still need IV gentamycin

7.     Repeat

 

Calculating the initial dose (calculator)

 

-        Use the NHS intranet calculator

-        Enter the patient’s weight, height and creatinine level

-        Do NOT use any other online calculators

-        Do NOT use eGFR

 

Alternatively….

 

Calculating the initial dose (manually)

 

1)     First work out the patient’s Creatinine Clearance (CrCl) using the formula below:

 

 

Cr/Cl = ([140- age (years) x weight (kg)] / serum creartinine) x 1.23 (male) or 1.04 (female)

 

 

-        If the pt is overweight, compare their Actual body weight (ABW) to the maximum body weight (MBW) for their height

·        The corresponding MBW for a patient’s height can be found in a table in the handbook

·        à Use whichever one is higher

·        This is because in this equation body weight is being used as a marker for lean body mass (ie muscle) which is what produces creatinine.

 

-        If their serum creatinine is very low i.e. <60 umol/L, use 60 in the calculation

-        Be aware this equation can overestimate renal function in elderly or malnourished patients

 

*DO NOT USE eGFR because the units for eGFR and CrCl are different – CrCl is normalised to surface area

 

 

The online calculator automatically does all of this for you!

 

2)     Once you have worked out the CrCl, use the dosing table in the handbook to work out an appropriate initial dose based on the pt’s CrCl and their ACTUAL body weight

 

-        The actual body weight is used here because the volume of distribution that a drug dilutes into when administered into a patient includes their fat stores, not just their lean body mass. Therefore an obese patient will need a higher than a normal weight individual who has the same creatinine clearance.

-        The table also gives the dosing interval. This is either 24 or 48 hourly so be careful to prescribe it clearly on the kardex so the nurses know. (Cross out the days on which the patient is not to get a dose!)

 

 

 

Notes:

-        Using the online calculator is the safest method

-        However it is often a good idea to first quickly work out the patient’s dose by hand in order to gain a rough estimate of what the dose should be. That way you will be able to detect if you enter any incorrect information and the calculator produces a spurious result.

 

Prescribing Initial Dose of Gentamycin

 

-        Write up initial dose in regular section of kardex

-        If 48 hourly dosing, make sure to write clearly ‘Additional Instructions: 48 HOURLY’ and cross out the boxes for the days on which they are not due to get it

-        The recommended dose should be given by IV infusion over 30-60 minutes.

 

You can also write in the Additional instructions section:

-        “Max 3 days. Monitor levels and U+Es”

 

Monitoring Gentamicin concentrations

 

-        CONCENTRATIONS ARE MEANINGLESS UNLESS YOU HAVE AN ACCURATE TIME FOR BOTH WHEN THE DOSE WAS GIVEN AND WHEN THE BLOOD SAMPLE WAS TAKEN

-        Take a sample 6-14 hours after the start of the first infusion

-        Then routinely, 6-14 hours after a dose every 2-3 days

-        Monitor conc more commonly if unstable renal function

-        Need daily U+Es – if renal function changes the dose needs to change

-        If >3 days - must speak to Micro

-        If >7 days – need formal audiology testing. Warn patient to look out for signs of ototoxicity – tinnitus, deafness, nauseas

-        Record the time of the sample accurately on the blood form and on the sheet at the end of the patient’s bed

-        When the lab returns the concentration, plot it on the graph

·        If the level is below the first line – give the same dose again

·        If the level is between the 2 lines – give the same dose but at 48 hours

·        If the level is above the top line – withhold the dose completely and remeasure the concentration the next day (check the patient’s renal function hasn’t changed!)

 

Note: this graph does not detect under-dosing. If the patient is failing to respond to treatment speak to Microbiology or pharmacy. Remember to check the patient’s renal function, if it has improved recently then they will be clearing the drug faster and will therefore need their dose increased!

Rationale for Gentamycin monitoring

 

For genatmycin you want high peaks and low troughs. This is because the side-effects are linked to the trough levels. So when you measure a genatmicin level at 6-14 hours post dose, you are wanting it to be under the line to ensure the patient is not at risk of side-effects.

The killing power of gentamicin depends on a high peak. Unlike vancomycin though, gentamycin is still effective despite low troughs because it has something called ‘post-antibiotic effect’ meaning that gentamicin still kills bacteria even when its serum concentration levels have fallen considerably as long as the initial peak was high enough to allow good penetrance.

 

 

Stopping Gentamicin

 

-        Ideally patients should never receive more than 3 days of empirical gentamicin

-        After 3 days, ask yourself does the patient still need Gent?

-        Check culture results?

-        Switch to oral alternative?

 

Vancomycin

 

Vancomycin can be given either by

1)     Continuous infusion

2)     Pulsed infusion

 

Continuous infusions

 

Pulsed Infusions

 

-        More effective

-        Constant steady state concentration

-        They are preferred where practical,

-        especially for patients with deep-seated infections eg pneumonia, endocarditis, bone and joint infections

 

-        More commonly used

-        More practical for the patient

-        Allow one IV pump to be used for numerous patients

 

 

Always check with the nursing staff prior to prescribing to ensure they have enough IV pumps and they are a familiar with the type of administration.

Vancomycin - Pulsed infusion

 

Loading infusion

 

-        Vancomycin requires a loading dose.

-        Loading doses are calculated based on the patient’s weight alone

 

Understanding Loading Doses

Imagine the patient is a bath tub full of water, and you want to instantly achieve a set concentration in the water. The amount of drug to add depends on how much water there is i.e. the patient’s weight.

 

-        Using the table in the handbook, plot the patient’s Actual body weight to work out the loading dose and the duration of the infusion.

 

-        Prescribe the loading dose in the once only section of the kardex.

·        Make sure to write how long the infusion should take ‘Infuse over 90 mins’

 

NOTE:

-        If vancomycin is given too fast à RED MAN SYNDROME

·        Histamine release à flushing, tachycardia, can progress to arrest

 

 

Pulsed Infusion – Maintenance Dose

 

-        Depends on CrCl alone

 

Understanding Maintenance Doses

Again, imagine the patient is a bath tub full of water and you have managed to reach a set concentration in the water after your loading dose. Now imagine that the plug is open and water is leaking out. The tap is also running though, so the total volume of water remains the same. The maintenance dose is the amount you need to keep adding to make up for the drug that you are losing down the plughole (the kidneys).

The patient’s renal function determines how fast water is leaving through the plug hole. The maintenance dose is the rate at which you need to keep giving the drug to maintain the concentration.

 

 

-        Use the online calculator to work out the maintenance dose

-        Or work out the patient’s creatinine clearance by hand and use the table in the handbook to find the correct dose

-        Dosing can be at 12, 24 or 48 hours after the loading dose depending on renal function

 

Monitoring pulsed infusion

 

-        Take a trough sample

·        ie take a blood sample just before the patient is due their next dose

-        Repeat trough sample every 2-3 days if stable

-        Check U+Es daily

-        Record the time of the last dose and sample accurately

 

Target trough concentration: 10-20mg/L

(for MRSA and certain infections (eg bone and soft tissue), the target concentration is at the higher end of this range 15-20mg/L)

When the sample comes back, if trough concentration….

·        <10 à increase dose (by 50% or seek advice)

·        10-20 à maintain present dose

·        >20 à stop until <20mg/L then seek advice

 

Vancomycin continuous infusion

 

Loading infusion

 

-        same as for pulsed.

 

Continuous infusion - Maintenance Dose

 

-        Start immediately after loading dose infusion is given

-        Based on CrCl

-        Use online calculator

-        Or work out CrCl by hand and use the table in the handbook

-        Will also need to fill in an infusion chart for the nurses

 

Monitoring Continuous Infusions

 

-        Take a sample 12-24 hours after starting continuous infusion

-        Then at any point every 2-3 days

-        NOTE TARGET CONC IS DIFFERENT

 

è Target concentration: 15-25mg/L

 

Rationale for Vancomycin Monitoring

 

For vancomycin it is only the trough level that you are interested in. This is because treatment failure depends on the trough level. For vancomycin to work, you want the concentration to never fall below the MIC (minimal inhibitory concentration) for the particular bacteria you are trying to kill. So when you measure a Vancomycin level at 24-48 hours you want it the level to be within the target range.

 

 

Main learning points:

 

-        Always ask does this patient need IV antibiotics?

-        Monitor U+Es - if a patient’s renal function changes (either way) their dose will need to be altered

-        Drug concentrations cannot be interpretated without the exact time and date of both the dose and when the sample was taken.

-        Be careful never to mix up the vancomycin and gentamicin dosing calculator and tables – the doses are VERY different.

 

 

When prescribing any antibiotic you need to document

 

-        INDICATION

-        Intended duration of the course

-        If spoken to micro for advice (always write the name of the person you spoke to)

-        If deviated from policy and why

-        Dose calculation (for vanc and gent)

-        Severity score eg SIRS, CURB65
