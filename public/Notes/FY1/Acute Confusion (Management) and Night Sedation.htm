Managing Acutely Disturbed patients

 

-        It is important to remember that most acutely confused patients who become disruptive do NOT have a mental illness

-        Mental disturbance is often a sign of acute physical illness, which can be serious eg head-injury

-        Some disruptive patients are not unwell, and once physical illness has been excluded the police should be contacted via appropriate channels

 

When asked to see a disturbed patient as an FY1?

 

You are the FY1 on nights. You are called to see an agitated patient ‘who is strangling a nurse’. What do you do?

 

1) Safety – make sure all patients and staff are safe.

2) Calm the patient (sedate if necessary)

3) Investigate and treat the cause.

 

1)     Safety

 

2)     Calm the patient

 

-        Talk to the patient in a calm tone

-        Manage in a quiet well-lit room with familiar staff

-        If the patients is a risk to self or others à Emergency sedation!

·        But this should always be discussed with a senior prior to prescribing.

 

2 main options:

 

Ø  Haloperidol

§  Best avoided in elderly

§  Do not give to patients with Parkinson’s, lewy body dementia, Cardiac disorders or who have a history of seizures

§  Does 0.5mg O/IM (max 3mg)

 

Ø  Lorazepam

§  Contraindicated in respiratory failure  (respiratory suppression)

§  Dose 0.5mg IV/O/IM

§  Max 4mg if young and healthy

 

-        Use oral first line

·        It is very uncommon (and unpleasant) that you would have to give IM sedation while the patient is being restrained. Talk to the patient calmly and most will accept to take oral medication.

-        Start low and go slow – remember you can always give more but you cannot take back!

-        If you do give too much à Flumazenil (antedote)

-        Wait 30 mins between doses to monitor effect

-        Remember to ask the nurses (and document) that the patient will need to be closely monitored for respiratory suppression.

Prescribing Night Sedation

 

As an FY1 you will commonly be asked to prescribe night sedation for patients. However it is a tricky area with important things to watch out for.

 

-        If the patient is on regular night sedation in the community you can continue the prescription PRN (if appropriate)

-        If the patient does not regularly take night sedation you should try to avoid prescribing it wherever possible.

-        Look for and treat possible modifiable causes – i.e. control pain, treat depression, alter drugs (eg move SSRI earlier in the day)

-        If it is necessary to prescribe discuss pros (better sleep) and cons (possible dependence) of night sedation with patient – explain it is only a short term temporary fix

 

For night sedation you want to prescribe a short acting benzodiazepine in the once only section:

 

è Temazepam oral 10-20mg nocte (short acting)

 

If any concerns as to the patient eg elderly, low body weight, hepatic impairment, drug/alcohol use, prescribe a smaller dose – 5mg temazepam

 

Note:

 

-        Never prescribe sedation to patients with head injury – need to be able to assess their GCS.

-        Never prescribe benzodiazepines in patients with severe respiratory disease – benzodiazepines are GABA agonists à respiratory depression

-        Never prescribe if severe hepatic impairment

-        Caution in the elderly

-        Caution if co-prescribed other CNS depressants (eg opiates)

-        Patients should never be discharged home on their night sedation (and there have been medico-legal cases over patients who became dependant on their night sedation)

-        Abrupt withdrawal of benzodiazepines after prolonged use can cause confusion, seizures, psychosis.
