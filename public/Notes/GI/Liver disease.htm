Liver Disease

 

Signs of Liver Disease

 

·        Jaundice

·        Encephalopathy

·        Hepatic flap

·        Foetar hepaticus

·        Ascites – due to decreased albumin

·        Peripheral Oedema

·        Haemorrhage – due to varices or coagulopathy

·        Purpura – due to thrombocytopaenia/decreased clotting factors

·        Hepatomegaly – seen in hepatitis. NB liver is small in cirrhosis

·        Splenomegaly – due to back-up of pressure from portal hypertension

·        Spider naevi  - >5 above nipples (SVC distribution) implies chronic disease; occur due to decreased oestrogen metabolism, seen on hands, chest (above diaphragm) and back. Fill from centre out (unlike telangectasia).

·        Palmar erythema – due to ↓ oestrogen metabolism; indicative of hyper-dynamic circulation (also seen in pregnancy, thyrotoxicosis, RA)

·        Loss of body hair – due to ↓ oestrogen metabolism

·        Leuconycia – white marks on nails; due to hypoalbuminaemia

·        Gynacomastia – due to oestrogen or spironolactone therapy

·        Clubbing – not common

·        Dupuytren’s contracture – seen in alcoholic cirrhosis

·        Xanthomas – occur in PBC

·        Scratch marks – due to itch from bile salts (cholestasis)

·        Testicular atrophy - due to ↓ oestrogen metabolism

·        Pale stools and dark urine – if cholestatic. Stools rarely pale if intrahepatic cause

·        Caput medusa – dilated veins around umbilicus

 

 

Acute Liver Disease

 

The most common causes of acute liver disease:

-        Acute viral hepatitis (see specific causes of liver disease)

-        Fulminant Hepatic Failure

-        Acute-on-chronic liver disease – i.e. decompensation of Chronic Liver disease

 

Fulminant Hepatic Failure

 

-        Occurs in previously normal livers

-        Commonly following paracetemol overdose

-        Can occur after acute viral hepatitis (rare)

-        Patient is jaundiced and commonly develops coagulopathy.

-        At high risk of encephalopathy.

-        Can be hyper-acute onset < 7 days, acute 1-6 weeks, sub acute >6-8 weeks

-        Aim to PREVETN if paracetamol OD = N-acetyl cysteine

·        Normally glutathione binds paracetemol. If OD, glutathionine stores are exhausted à pararcetemol toxic to liver

-        TRANSPLANT is the main treatment once FHF is established

·        If coagulopathy (INR>2) or encephalopathy à transplant!

-        Complication of FHF – cerebral oedema.

-        Patients with FHF awaiting transplant are very sick – keep in ITU.

 

Acute on Chronic Liver Disease

 

-        Occurs in patients with chronic liver disease (i.e. decompensation)

-        Most have precipitating factors eg Bleed, Sepsis, Dehydration, biochemical abnormality due to diuretic sensitivity

-        Investigate & treat precipitating cause eg Gastric tube – is there blood? Blood cultures? Ascites aspirate?

 

[For investigations for acute liver disease see below]

 

Chronic Liver Disease

 

·        Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease/steatosis

·        Alcohol

·        Autoimmune (CAH; PBC)                                                      

·        Viral Hepatitis (B; C)                                                               > Cirrhosis!

·        Drugs

·        Hereditary (Heamochromatosis; Wilson’s)

 

Liver disease is chronic if > 6months

-        Needs to be > 6monmths because it takes this long for LFTs to return to normal even after acute episode

 

Chronic Liver Disease has 2 clinical stages:

1)     Compensated – LFTs are deranged but no signs and symptoms; even with the reduced functional volume the remaining healthy liver tissue is able to perform the necessary functions.

2)     Decompensated – liver disease is extensive enough to manifest signs and symptoms (jaundice, ascites, encephalopathy, coagulopathy)

 

Liver Cirrhosis

 

Liver cirrhosis is a final common pathway that can result from any chronic or severe liver disease. Similarly many patients with chronic liver disease (eg Alcoholic Liver disease) never develop cirrhosis. It is not understood why some patients get it and others do not. Cirrhosis is a pathological term that describes the scarred liver tissue. Once cirrhosis has occurred it is irreversible.

 

Cirrhosis is characterised by:

-        Destruction of hepatocytes

-        Fibrosis and nodular regeneration

 

There are 2 types of Cirrhosis:

i)                 Micronodular – eg due to alcohol; often progressive cause

ii)                Macronodular – eg after acute hepatitis

 

 

Decompensated Liver Disease

 

1)     Ascites

2)     Encephalopathy

3)     Varices

 

1)    Ascites

 

-        Diagnose clinically: Shifting Dullness (however this requires 1-1.5litres to be detectable)

-        Diagnose on Abdominal US (earlier)

-        ALWAYS take an aspirate sample of the ascites fluid

·        Transudate          

·        <25g/litre, low LDH

·        Due to systemic causes

·        Most common = LF: Portal Hypertension + low albumin

·        Other causes:

·        Hypoalbuminaemia due to proteinuria from nephrotic syndrome

·        Constrictive pericarditis

 

·        Exudate (rare!)

·        >25g/litre, high LDH  (>200U/l)

·        Due to local causes

·        Malignancy – eg mets from ovarian/pelvic tumour

·        Infection (TB)

 

Also measure:

-        WBCs in the fluid, if WCC >500 mm3 or Neutrophils >250 = Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis

·        Very serious complication of ascites; presents with abdominal pain, peritonitis and s/s of sepsis; can lead to death. Consider in any patient with ascites who becomes acutely unwell.  Treat with broad spec antibiotics (eg IV augmentin). Results from translocation of gut flora through the bowel wall and into ascetic fluid causing peritonitis and sepsis.

 

-        Serum-ascetic albumin gradient (SAAG). This is the difference between serum Albumin and conc of albumin in the ascites fluid. If SAAG > 11/g = portal hypertension! (NB In chronic renal failure albumin decreases so globulin levels in the serum rise)

 

-        lymphocytes present in the ascites fluid = TB?

 

So send ascites aspirate to:

 

Ø  Biochemistry            

·        Transudate vs exudates

·        If SAAG >11 à due to portal hypertension

 

Ø  Microbiology

·        WCC > 250 (or neutrophils >250) = SBP! Culture & Start IV antibiotics

·        Culture

 

Ø  Cytology

·        Malignant cells?

 

 

If new ascites, also get Ultrasound to screen for hepatocellular carcinoma.

 

 

Management of Ascites:

 

If severe (ie distension affecting breathing) – drain.

Ø  Can drain up to 20 litres! Can drain ascites in one go (unlike pleural effusion). Need to give one vial of IV albumin for every 1.5litres that you drain in order to prevent hypotension.

 

For moderate ascites:

·        Bed rest

·        Restrict Na (2g/day) & Water intake (1.5-2l/day)

·        Laxatives – constipation can precipitate encephalopathy

·        Diuretics – both – Loop (Furosemide) & K-sparing (Spironolactone)

·        Monitor weight daily (more practical than fluid balance chart)

·        aim for ~ 1lb/day weight loss

 

2 clinical situations can result from treatment:

Diuretic sensitive – can develop worrying low Na+ conc due to diuresis and dilution of Na+ concentration due to fluid oedema.

Diuretic Resistant – diuretics have no effect.

If management is unsuccessful it may be necessary to perform a Trans-jugular Intra-hepatic Portal Systemic Shunt or TIPSS Procedure.

-        Invasive procedure that bypasses blood from the portal vein straight into the hepatic vein missing out the liver

-        Indications:

·        Resistant Ascites

·        Resistant Variceal bleeding

-        Not suitable for patients with Fulminant hepatic failure or severe encephalopathy!

-        Catheter is inserted in the jugular vein à SVC à IVC à Hepatic vein. Then make a small puncture in Portal vein and place a metal stent to decrease portal pressure

-        Can lead to increased incidence of Encephalopathy however.

-        Used as a therapeutic bridge between Transplant for patients with chronic liver disease – means patient doesn’t have to come in daily to have their ascites drained

 

Liver Transplant

 

The only cure for cirrhosis.

Donor livers are very scarce; most are cadaveric.

Need to have stopped drinking ≥ 6 months to even be considered for transplant.

Require lifelong immunosuppression post-transplant ( eg ciclosporin; tacrolimus)

 

5 year post-transplant survival ~70-85%

 

Split-liver Live donation – remove a large part of the right lobe from the donor (remaining liver regenerates). Possible, but rarely done due to high donor mortality. More used for children who require transplant because they require a smaller portion of liver so less risk to donor.

 

 

2)    Encephalopathy

 

Signs of Encephalopathy:

·        Flap

·        Confusion/↓cognition

·        Constructive apraxia – unable to draw shapes

·        Foetar Hepaticus – a sweet but faecal smell

 

 

-        Encephalopathy can occur in:

·        Fulminant Hepatic Failure

·        Decompensation of chronic Liver Disease

§  Most have precipitating factors eg Bleed, Sepsis, Dehydration, biochemical abnormality due to diuretic sensitivity

§  Investigate & treat precipitating cause eg Gastric tube – is there blood? Blood cultures? Ascites aspirate?

§  Also give LACTULOSE (20mg tds) – non-absorbable carbohydrate, laxative; constipation can precipitate encephalopathy in patients with chronic liver failure.

 

Grades of Encephaolpathy

 

1 – confusion, hand flap, +/- EEG changes

2 – Drowsy

3 – unconscious but can be roused

4 – Coma

 

 

3)    Varices

 

-        90% of pts with cirrhosis develop varices however only 30% of these will ever bleed

-        Regular endoscopic screening for presence of varices

-        If small varices → start on Propanolol (decreases portal pressure)

-        If medium - large varices – start on propanolol and enter into variceal eradiation programme (eg Endoscopic Band ligation)

-        If present acutely with haematemeis follow emergency management protocol (see notes on haematemesis)

 

Other complications of Liver Disease:

 

Coagulopathy

 

-        Serious complication, especially if varices!

-        The liver produces clotting factors, so coagulopathies are common.

-        Monitor coag! May require transfusion of Fresh Frozen Plasma

 

Thrombocytopaenia

 

-        Portal hypertension à back pressure to spleen à splenomegaly à hypersplenism à decreased platelets, as the platelets all sequester in the enlarged spleen

-        Can also result from direct toxic effect of alcohol on bone marrow

 

Hypoglycaemia

 

-        The liver is a major store of glycogen and also plays an integral role in glucose metabolism

 

Hepato-renal syndrome (HRS)

 

-        Complication of end-stage liver disease

-        liver failure produces renal failure in healthy kidneys

-        Can occur in chronic or acute liver failure

-        Occurs due to decreased peripheral vascular volume due to ascites leading to pre-renal failure

-        ALSO portal hypertension is thought to alter blood flow and vessel tone from the kidneys (even though the renal vein does not contribute to the portal vein)

-        Also phenomenon where reduced hepatic clearance of toxins directly damages kidneys

-        The kidneys themselves have no pathology

-        May be precipitated by anything that lowers BP eg Infection, haemorrhage

-        Presents with ↑ Ct, oligouria and worsening ascites

-        Can be acute (type 1; ~2 weeks) or chronic onset (type 2)

-        Diagnose by excluding other causes of RF

-        Survival:         

§  Type 1 = 10%

§  Type 2 = median 6 months

-        IV albumin may reduce incidence

 

 

Specific Causes of Liver Disease

 

Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD)

 

Most common cause of Chronic Liver Disease in the West is Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD).

This is largely benign and has few or no signs other than deranged LFTs.

NAFLD – the presence of Fat in the hepatocytes – can progress however onto a form of Hepatitis known as Non-Alcohol Steatosis (NASH).

NASH is a more severe form of disease that can progress onto fibrosis and ultimately cirrhosis.

NAFLD → NASH (inflammation around the fatty hepatocytes) → Fibrosis → Cirrhosis.

Consumption of alcohol >20g/day automatically excludes NAFLD.

The main cause is OBESITY. Can result from other causes eg Drugs, amiodarone.

NAFLD is associated with insulin resistance. It is thought that measures effective in treating T2DM (eg weight loss, exercise, metformin) may be effective in treating NAFLD.

 

Alcoholic Liver Disease

 

Pathology

 

The term alcoholic liver disease encompasses a spectrum of pathology that can result from alcohol excess.

 

Fatty change → Alcoholic Hepatitis → Alcoholic Cirrhosis

 

Only 10-20% of heavy drinkers will develop cirrhosis. It is unknown why some drinkers are susceptible to cirrhosis and others are somewhat protected.

 

Fatty Change

 

-      invariably occurs with alcohol excess

-      changes are reversible on stopping drinking

-      present to minimal degree even after moderate quantities of alcohol

-      mainly in zone 3 of liver acinus

-      Metabolism of alcohol increases NADH/NAD ratio – decreases redox potential → increased hepatic fatty acid synthesis + decreased fatty acid oxidation → accumulation of fatty acids which are esterified to TGs.

-      cells become swollen with fat

-      steatosis can also occur in obesity, diabetes, starvation and chronic illness

-      can lead to fibrosis if collagen is laid down around the central hepatic veins. This can eventually escalate to cirrhosis, even without a preceding hepatitis. Occurs due to the direct effects of alcohol on stellate cells, transforming them into collagen-producing myofibroblast cells.

 

Clinical features of Fatty Changes:

 

-      Often asymptomatic

-      Mild elevation in LFTs

-      Vague abdominal symptoms eg nausea, D+V, are due to direct effects of alcohol on GI system

-      Hepatomegaly can be present

 

 

Alcoholic Hepatitis

 

-      Fatty change + infiltration by WBCs (mainly neutrophils) ie Inflammation

-      Hepatocyte necrosis occurs in zone 3

-      Mallory bodies (dense cytoplasmic inclusions) can be seen in hepatocytes (also seen in Wilson’s and PBC)

-      Can progress to cirrhosis

 

Clinical features of Alcoholic Hepatitis

 

Varying clinical presentation:

-      Patient can be well – hepatitis only present on liver biopsy along with fatty changes

-      Moderate illness – jaundice plus other symptoms of chronic liver. LFTs are deranged. Diagnosed clinically on symptoms + blood tests (or on liver histology).

-      Severe illness – can occur on top of underlying cirrhosis. Fever (due to liver necrosis), pain, jaundice, ascites, hepatomegaly, coagulopathy plus any other signs of chronic liver disease may be present. In severe alcohol hepatitis patients can have changes of decompensated liver disease even if there is no cirrhosis present

 

 

Alcoholic Cirrhosis

 

-      Chronic injury to liver leads to activation of stellate cells. These secrete collagen in the Space of Disse leading to fibrosis. The collagen blocks the sinusoidal fenestrations thus decreasing liver function.

-      Amidst the fibrosis, the hepatocytes continue to regeneration in a classically ‘micronodular’ pattern

 

Clinical features of Alcoholic Cirrhosis

 

-      Can be compensated or decompensated

-      If compensated, the residual viable tissue is sufficient to carry out function and so very few s/s are present

-      Decompensated = ascites, encephalopathy, variceal bleeding, coagulopathy, jaundice

 

 

 

Investigations:

 

-        Bloods

·        LFTs - ↑AST and ALT. ↓albumin (implies decreased synthetic function of liver)

·        GGT - ↑ if recent drinking

·        FBC - ↓Hb + ↑MCV (macrocytic anaemia due to B12 and folate deficiency), ↑WCC (in hepatitis), ↓platelets

·        Coag - ↑PT

 

-        Ultrasound

·        Will show fatty change, ascites, cirrhosis, hepatomegaly or shrunken liver if cirrhosis. Also screens for HCC (However if ↑ AFP it is necessary to do a more sensitive scan eg CT or MRI).

-        Ascitic tap

·        For biochemistry, microbiology and cytology

 

-        CT/MRI

·        Useful if ?HCC, collateral varices.

 

-        Endoscopy – for detection and treatment of varices

 

-        Liver Biospy – not routinely done in alcoholic liver disease but can confirm severity and type of liver disease. Stain for iron and copper if any doubt as to aetiology. Need to make sure coag is OK prior to biopsy.

 

 

Management and Prognosis

 

-        Stop drinking

-        Treat Withdrawal/Delerium Tremens (see below)

-        IV thiamine to prevent Wernicke-korsakoff syndrome (then oral thiamine)

-        Bed rest

-        High protein diet with supplementary vitamins (this is due to high rate of malnutrition amongst liver disease patients). If encephalopathic, the patient may not be able to tolerate high protein – give what they can manage.

-        Monitor coagulation

-        Corticosteroids should be given depending on GAHS.

·        But NOT if co-existing infection is a contraindication!

Viral hepatitis

 

Hepatitis:

 

           A = acute

           C = chronic

           B = both

 

-        B+C can progress to cirrhosis

-        Also increase risk of hepatocellular carcinoma

-        Hepatitis D only occurs in conjuction with Hep B infection

 

Hepatitis A Virus

 

-        Acute viral infection (NOT chronic)

-        Mainly affects children and young adults

-        Esp if travel history to endemic countries (it is not common in the UK)

-        Spread by food and drink/oro-faecal route (NOT blood borne)

-        Vaccination available – lasts 25 years if booster vaccine given within 12 months of first vaccine

 

Clinical features:

 

-        3-6 weeks incubation time

-        Prodromal symptoms:

·        Lethargy

·        Nauseas & vomiting

-        Hepatitis symptoms:

·        Jaundice

·        Hepatomegaly

·        RUQ pain

 

Investigations:

 

-        Suspect after history + deranged LFTs

-        Confirm diagnosis with serology – Hep A Virus antibody

 

Management:

 

-        Conservative.

-        Monitor coag

-        Self-resolving

 

Hepatitis B

 

-        Can be acute or chronic

-        It is a Blood borne virus

·        Endemic in many countries

·        Risk factors: IVDU, sexual promiscuity, travel history, tattoos, piercings, foreign medical treatment, pre1987 blood transfusion, or a sexual partner with anyone with these risk factors

 

-        Vaccination available

·        Given to high risk individuals (eg healthcare workers, IVDU, sex workers)

 

Clinical features:

 

-        Incubation time – long, 1-6 months

-        Similar presentation to Hep A:

·        Prodromal symptoms:

§  Lethargy

§  Nauseas & vomiting

·        Hepatitis symptoms:

§  Jaundice

§  Hepatomegaly

§  RUQ pain

-         + extra hepatic features eg - urticaria + arthralgia

 

Investigations:

 

Serology:

-        HB-C – Core Ag – detectable early and persists for life. Implies previous or ongoing hep B infection.

-        HB- S – Surface Ag – if present for >6months à chronic infection

-        HB-E – only present if super-infection (very infectious)

 

-        IgM anti-HBc – occurs in acute infection. If present implies <6 months since exposure

-        Anti-HBs – Hep B Surface Antibody – implies previous exposure and immunity or effective vaccination

 

 

Management:

 

-        Post-exposure prophylaxis

·        INTERFERON-alpha – subcut injection twice weekly for 6 weeks

·        Give vaccination

-        Primary treatment

·        Supportive

·        Avoid alcohol

·        Vaccinate sexual contacts

·        Interferon and other antivirals may have a role in chronic disease

 

Prognosis:

 

-        Some patients clear infection

-        Proportion of patients (~5-10% adults; much higher incidence in children) never clear infection à chronic liver disease

-        1% develop fulminant hepatic failure

 

Hepatitis C

 

-        Common Blood borne virus

-        Major cause of liver transplantation

-        CHRONIC INFECTION

 

Clinical Features:

 

-        Presents late, with signs of CHRONIC liver disease

-        Risk of cirrhosis ↑↑ if also drinks alcohol

 

Investigations:

 

-        Confirm diagnosis by HC antibodies (via PCR)

-        Liver biopsy if serology is inconclusive

 

Management:

 

-        Interferon-alpha

-        Ribavirin

-        50% success rate with treatment (ie 50% remain long term carriers)

-        30% develop cirrhosis in 20 years

-        Increased risk of hepatocellular carcinoma

 

Genetic Causes of Liver Disease

 

Haemachromatosis

 

-        Genetic

·        Autosomal recessive

·        Common, 1 in 10 are carriers in Scotland

-        Mutation in the HFE gene à ↑ iron absorption from the gut. This leads to ↑ depsotiion of free iron in the liver à TOXIC     

-        Also deposits in joints (arthritis), pancreas (diabetes), heart and pituitary

-        ↑ risk of cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma

-        However treatment is effective if diagnosis is made

 

Clinical features:

 

-        Usu presents 3-60 years (takes time for iron to build up)

-        Can occur in children

-        More commonly presents in males (due to lack of menstruation)

-        Diverse group of symptoms depending where iron is deposited:

·        Fatigue

·        Mood swings, depression - brain

·        Arthritis, commonly knuckles - joints

·        Sexual dysfunction (erectile dysfunction or amenorrhoea) - pituitary

·        CardiomyopathyHeart failure/arrthymias - heart

·        Diabetes - pancreas

·        Liver disease/RUQ pain -liver

·        Bronzing of the skin/permanent ‘tan’ - skin

 

Investigations:

 

-        Usu an incidental finding on FBC - ↑Hb

-        Then test iron studies: Free iron (↑), iron binding capacity (↓), trasnferrin saturation (↑), ferritin (↑)

-        If concern over relatives being affected, a simple blood test can be used to identify the HFE gene mutation using PCR. The same mutation is present in >90% of cases.

 

Management:

 

-        VENESECTION

·        removal of 1 pint of blood

·        2x/week for 2 years

·        Then 3-4x/year after that

 

 

Wilson’s Disease

 

-        Genetic: autosomal recessive

-        Rare

-        Mutation in the gene responsible for a COPPER transport protein, results in  ↑ copper deposition in liver and CNS (especially the basal ganglia)

-        Copper is normally absorbed in proximal small intestine. In the liver copper is incorporated in caeruloplasmin, which can then be excreted in bile if there is excess copper. In Wilson’s disease, copper absorption from the GIT is normal but it is not then incorporated into caeruloplasmin. This results in free copper accumulating in the liver and other organs.

 

Clinical features:

 

-        Usu presents in childhood

·        *suspect Wilsons in all children with abnormal LFTs

 

-        CNS s/s: behavioural changes, tremor, dysarthria, dementia, parkinsonism

 

-        Kayser-flescher Rings

·        copper deposition in Iris

·        distinguish from corneal arcus by looking for copper particles on ophthalmoscopy

 

 

Investigations:

 

-        LOW CAERULOPLASMIN

-        Low total serum copper, but ↑ free serum copper

-        ↑ urinary excretion of copper (24 hour collection)

-        PCR for gene mutation

-        Liver biopsy - ↑ hepatic copper

-        MRI – basal ganglia degeneration ± frontotemporal, cerebrellar and brainstem atrophy

 

Management:

 

-        Chelate with penicillamine (lifelong)

·        s/e of penicillamine – rash, marrow suppression (neutropaenia, anaemia), Lupus.

-        If early à changes are reversible

-        Sadly CNS signs are often irreversible

-        May require liver transplant

-        Also avoid copper rich foods (liver, chocolate, mushrooms)

-        Screen siblings for gene mutation

 

 

Alpha-1 Antitrypsin deficiency

 

-        Rare, autosomal dominant cause of liver disease

·        Although autosomal dominant, the gene is co-dominant menaing that each allele contributes 50% to the phenotype, therefore it is usually only clinically significant if homozygous for the gene mutation

-        Associated with emphysema

·        Suspect in any young patient with COPD and deranged LFTs.

·        Also consider it in older patients with severe progressive COPD.

-        Alpha-1 antitrypsin is a protease inhibitor, one of its main functions is inhibiting elastase enzyme released by neutrophils to prevent auto-destruction of tissues. If deficient in Alpha-1 antitrypsin, when neutrophils release elastase in response to cigarette smoke/infection, the elastase destroys the lung tissue à emphysema

-        Alpha-1 antitrypsin is produced in the liver and can also cause liver disease. In Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency the protein is produced but not released. Therefore it builds up in the hepatocytes and disturbs the cells function before eventually triggering to cell death.

 

 

 

Auto-immune Liver Disease

 

 

Auto-immune Hepatitis (AIH)

 

-        Aka Chronic Active Hepatitis (CAH)

-        Auto-antibodies again hepatocytes

-        Affects young-middle aged women

 

Clinical features:

 

-        Presents with hepatitis (can be acute or insidious)

·        Jaundice, RUQ pain, fever, hepatomegaly

-        ± signs of auto-immune disease

·        Fever, malaise, urticaria, polyarthritis, glomerulonephritis

 

-        Amenorrhoea is also common

 

Investigations:

 

-        LFTs abnormal

-        Auto-antibodies – ANCA, smooth muscle antibody (SMA), SLA

-        Liver biopsy

 

Diagnosis is inexact – there is a lot of overlap between PBC, PSC etc.

 

Management:

 

-        Steroids in acute setting

-        Azathioprine

-        Liver transplant

 

Prognosis:

-        10 year survival >80% overall (worse if cirrhosis at presentation)

-        Some patients go into remission with treatment

 

Primary Biliary Cirrhosis

 

-        Progressive auto-immune destruction of bile duct within the lvier

-        Bile accumulates in liver à hepatocyte damage à cirrhosis

-        90% are women

 

Clinical features:

 

-        Presents with:

·        Pruritis

·        Lethargy

·        RUQ tenderness

·        Xanthalasma

·        Jaundice

·        Incidental finding of  deranged LFTs

·        Following investigation for hypercholesterolaemia

-        Later develops s/s of chronic liver disease

 

Investigations:

 

-        LFTs ↑

-        Serology – Anti-mitochondrial antibodies (AMA) present in >90%

-        Coag screen – coagulopathy is common

-        Liver biopsy – confirms diagnosis (shows granulomas and cirrhosis)

 

 

Management:

 

-        Ursodeoxycholic acid – may slow disease progression

-        Symptomatic control:

·        Colestyramine ± rifampicin for the itch

·        Codeine phosphate for diarrhoea

-        Liver transplant

-        Also remember:

·        Osteoposis prevention

·        Vitamin supplements

 

Prognosis:

 

-        Mean survival <2 years from onset of jaundice

-        Transplant improves survival

 

Primary Scelorsing Cholangitis

 

-        Similar to PBC

-        Causes progressive destrucrtion of both intra+extra-hepatic bile ducts

-        Strong association with Ulcerative colitis

-        Uncommonly for auto-immune diseases it is more common in men with UC, than women (m:f = 2:1)

 

Clinical features:

 

-        Similar presentation as PBC – itch, lethargy, RUQ pain, abnormal LFTs

-        Can present with picture similar to gallstones if extra-hepatic bile duct stricture i.e. obstructive jaundice (RUQ pain, pale stools, dark urine).

 

Investigations

 

-        Diagnosis made on MRCP

-        Liver biopsy

-        Auto-antibodies – AMA –ve (deifferentiates from PBC), but other auto-antibodies are commonly positive.

 

Management:

 

-        Endoscopic stenting of extra-hepatic bile ducts if large enough

-        Otherwise treat as PBC:

·        Ursodeoxycholic acid – may slow disease progression

·        Symptomatic control:

§  Colestyramine ± rifampicin for the itch

§  Codeine phosphate for diarrhoea

·        Liver transplant

·        Also remember:

§  Osteoposis prevention

§  Vitamin supplements

-        Also need to screen annually for colorectal cancer (due to UC; colonscopy) and cholangiocarcinoma (ultrasound).

 

Other causes of Liver Disease

 

Budd-Chiari Syndrome

 

-        Occlusion of hepatic vein

-        Triad of abdo pain, ascites and hepatomegaly

-        Can be acute or chronic

-        Risk factors of thrombosis: thrombophilia, OCP, previous DVT, smoking etc

-        Investigations: Ultrasound

-        Management: Anticoagulation

 

Drugs

 

-        Don’t forget drugs as a common cause for liver disease.

-        They can derange LFTs, cause jaundice, and even cause cirrhosis.

 

Common offenders include:

 

·        Methotrexate – hepatic fibrosis

·        Amiodarone – hepatic fibrosis

·        Rifampicin – heaptocellular toxicity

·        Isoniazid – heaptocellular toxicity

·        Erythromycin – cholestatic hepatitis/obstructive jaundice

·        Amoxicillin/augmentin – cholestatic hepatitis/obstructive jaundice

·        Anabolic steroids – cholestatis/obstructive jaundice. ↑HCC risk.

·        Paracetamol overdose

·        Aspirin – Reye syndrome

 

 

Investigation of Acute Liver Disease

 

If a patient presents with new liver disease…

 

History

 

-        Alcohol

-        Drugs (illicit but also amiodarone, methotraxate, antibiotics?)

-        FH – Wilson’s, haemochromatosis. Gilbert’s disease (minor congenital abnormality in haemoglobin metabolism resulting in chronic mild jaundice).

-        Hepatitis risks - tattoos, piercings, IVDU, sexual history, travel history, blood transfusions pre 1987, vertical transmission in endemic areas eg South East Asia, foreign medical treatment.

-        Pain – gallstones? Painless à ?head of pancreas carcinoma

 

Blood Tests:

 

General:

FBC     – ?haemochromatosis

LFTs    – cholestatic? Bilirubin?

Coag   – check severity of LF

Glucose – altered hepatic metabolism of carbohydrates

U+Es   – monitor for baseline; hepto-renal syndrome

AFP     – HCC cancer marker

 

Diagnostic Tests:

·        PBC – anti-mitochondrial antibodies in 95%; ANA in 35%; other auto-Abs

·        CAH – auto-Abs eg antinuclear antibody, anti-smooth muscle antibody, antimitochondrial antibody, antiphospholipid antibodies

·        Viral Hepatitis – serology for viral markers

·        Wilson’s  - ↓ serum Caeruloplasmin levels; ↑ hepatic copper levels; + Kayser-Fleischer rings,neurological signs etc

·        Haemochromatosis – serum ferritin; transferrin saturation level

 

If no cause is apparent then you can perform a liver screen which consists of…

 

-        Hepatitis Serology – HBV, HCV

-        Iron Studies – ferritin, TIBC, HFE

-        Alpha-1-antitrypsin

-        Caeruloplasmin

-        Auto-antibodies – ANA, AMA, ASMA, antiphospholipid

-        Alpha-feto protein

 

Also arrange an abdominal ultrasound!

 

 

Imaging

 

Ultrasound – rule out obstruction eg stones

MRI – eg for neurological lesions in Wilson’s

Biopsy

 

Liver biopsy – stage disease (eg PBC)

Also:

 

Ascitic tap – transudate vs exudates, ?SBP. ?malignancy
