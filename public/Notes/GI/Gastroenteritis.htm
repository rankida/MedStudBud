Gastroenteritis

 

-        Characterised by diarrhoea ±vomiting ± nausea ± abdo pain

-        Diarrhoea = increased fluidity ± frequency of bowel movements

 

Mechanisms of Diarrhoea:

 

-        Osmotic – hypertonic lumen draws in water eg malabsroption

-        Secretory –increase fluid secretion into lumen eg stimulates by infection

-        Inflammatory – mucosal destruction ↓absorption

-        Abnormal motility – both ↑ and ↓ motility

§  Decreased segmentation in small intestine à ↓gut transit time à ↓fluid reabsoprtion

§  Increased MMC in large bowel à increased delivery of fluid into rectum

 

Clinical Features:

 

-        Incubation periods:

·        Viruses: 1 day

·        Bacteria: hours-3 days

·        Parasites: 7-10 days

 

-        Blood increases likelihood of bacterial cause (campylobacter, salmonella, E O157)

-        Fever in adults increases likelihood of a bacterial cause (invasive)

-        Assess fluid status

-        Abdo exam – ALWAYS; could be appendicitis

 

Differential diagnosis

-        Inflammatory bowel disease

-        UTI – pyelonpehritis

-        Addison’s disease

-        Emesis gravidum – pregnancy test!

-        Constipation with overflow – esp in elderly patients, where solid faeces is impacted in the rectum with only liquid stools able to pass it

Investigations

 

-        Stool sample

·        Microscopy (include ova, cysts and parasites), culture and sensitivity.

·        Only necessary if severe or prolonged symptoms, or ?tropical illness, risk of outbreak (eg institution, daycare).

-        Bloods if unwell

-        If abdo distension à AXR and further imaging

 

Management

 

-        Low threshold for admitting infants and elderly

-        Rehydrate

·        Oral is usu sufficient

·        May need admission and IV fluids if severe/elderly

-        Limit spread – educate regarding hand hygiene etc

-        Start eating when appetite returns – small and bland

 

-        Anti-emetics if vomiting - although no evidence (avoid metoclopramide in children and young adults – EPSE)

 

-        Avoid antispasmodic and antidiarrhoeal drugs in children and if dysentery

·        Otherwise only use if traveling, have to return to work, up-coming special event.

 

-        In most cases Antibiotics are NOT required

·        Do not shorten length of symptoms (even if they rapidly erradicate bacteria from the stools) and may increase length of carriage

·        Only if systemically unwell, immunocomprimised or elderly

 

-        If you decide to give antibiotics Ciprofloxacin is most commonly used (however avoid in children). Effective for Campylobacter, Salmonella, Shigella.

 

-        AVOID WORK/SOCIAL EVENTS UNTIL 48hrs AFTER LAST LOOSE MOTION.

 

-        Notify public health

-        Both dysentery and food poising are notifiable diseases in UK

-        Often done by lab, however always check – it is YOUR duty

 

 

Complications

 

-        Dehydration – isotonic fluid loss

-        Metabolic acidosis (due to HCO3 loss from gut; may be balanced by H+ loss if vomiting also)

-        Hypokalaemia – loss in stools. Also depleted IV volume à aldosterone release à reabsorbs Na at expense of K

-        Renal failure (due to dehydration)

-        Reactive features (arthritis, carditis, urticaria, erythema nodosum, conjunctivitis, and Reiter's syndrome).

-        Salmonella can invade bones, joints, meninges, or the gallbladder.

-        Toxic megacolon is rare.

-        Some viruses may cause Guillain-Barre syndrome (also Campylobacter).

-        Poor absorption of drugs such as anticonvulsants or oral contraceptives may occur.

-        Death is uncommon and predominately in the elderly

 

Microbiology

 

-        Can be caused by bacteria, viruses and parasites

-        In adults usu bacterial cause (children - virus)

-        Often spread by food and drink

·        Either faeco-oral route contaminating food

·        Or insufficient preparation à food poisoning (insufficient cooking, storage, defrosting, reheating)

-        Most common causes in adults are: Campylobacter, Salmonella, Rotavirus, Norovirus, Giardia.

 

Bacteria

 

-        Invasive bacteria – bacteria enters enterocyte and causes ulceration and abscess formation (eg Salmonella, campylobacter, shigella, E.coli). Blood in the stool implies invasive cause. Non-invasive bacteria tend to be toxin-secreting.

 

Campylobacter

 

-        Common food associated pathogen

-        Source: chicken, meat

-        Prodromal fever then abdo pain, profuse watery diarrhoea + blood

-        Give erythromycin (or ciprofloxacin) if severe.

 

Salmonella

 

-        Common food associated pathogen

-        Due salmonella enteritidis

-        Usu from meat, chicken, eggs

-        Watery diarrhoea + blood

-        Can be severe

-        Can invade other tissues (uncommon) – meningitis, osteomyelitis, endocarditis.

 

E. coli

 

-        Many types; can be part of normal flora

-        Can be toxin releasing or invasive

-        E0157 à Haemolytic Uraemic Syndrome

 

Shigella

 

-        Due to shigella bacterium

-        Causes dysentery (bloody diarrhoea)

 

Cholera

 

-        Due to vibrio cholera (uncommon now; still endemic in some developing countries)

-        Contaminated drinking water and sewage exposure

-        Profuse watery diarrhoea + blood

-        Rx: tetracyclines

 

Clostridium difficile

 

-        Can be part of normal gut flora

-        Risk factors: elderly, institution, hospital, recent broad spec antibiotic use

-        à severe diarrhoea (no blood)

-        à pseudomemraneous colitis

-        It is due to an anaerobic bacteria.

-        Produces cyto-skelaton altering toxins à loosens tight junctions à paracellular water loss

-        Also produces spores (can survive in environment)

-        Ix: Toxin assay – Taken from stool sample. Result the next day.

-        Stool culture takes longer

 

-        Rx:  

·        Isolate the patients/infection control

·        Discontinue offending antibiotics

·        Anti-anerobic agent

§  à Metrinidazole (if non-severe)

§  à Vancomycin

 

Note: alcohol hand gel is ineffective at removing spores, therefore you need to WASH YOUR HANDS with soap and running water after seeing a patient with loose stools. It is actually the running water that mechanically removes the spores from your hands!

 

 

Protozoa

 

Cryptosporidium

 

-        Protozoa

-        Sheep faeces in water

-        Ix: stool microscopy for ova

-        Usu self-limiting.

 

Giardia

 

-        Protozoa

-        Causes persistent diarrhoea and malabsorption

-        Endemic in many countries; can occur in developed countries too

-        Usu consider in patients with +ve travel history or in any patient whose symptoms fail to improve

-        Rx: metrinidazole

 

Viral

 

-        Rotavirus – most common cause in children

-        Adenovirus

-        Norovirus – winter vomiting virus outbreaks

 

 

Gastroenteritis in Children

 

-        Usu self-limiting, however in infants lower body weight and inability to concentrate urine means need to monitor closely

-        Most commonly viral under age 5 (70%) eg rotavirus, norovirus

-        Bloody diarrhoea raises suspicion of bacterial infection (esp E. coli O157 and salmonella)

-        Fever is common in all gastroenteritis in children

 

Differential Diagnosis

 

-        Not all D+V in children is due to gastroenteritis

-        Need to rule out other infection

·        Eg: UTI, otitis media, meningitis, pneumonia

-        Intussecption – “red-currant jelly stool” may be reported as bloody diarrhoea

-        Pyloric stenosis – if projectile vomiting

-        DKA

 

Management:

 

-        Oral Rehydration therapy

-        5mls dioralyte every 5 mins

-        Can be managed at home or mums are given a syringe and stopclock in A+E

-        IV fluids if severe

 

-        Avoid antidiarrhoeals

 

-        Admit if:

·        <1 and severe

·        Getting worse

·        Abdominal mass

·        Localised abdominal tenderness

·        Anuria

 

-        Most cases do not require antibiotics!

-        Exceptions are signs of sepsis, cholera, shigella, giardiasis, amoebiasis, typhoid fever.

 

 

Extra Notes:

 

Haemolytic Uraemic Syndrome

 

-        Most common cause of acute renal failure in children

-        Linked to infection with E. coli O157 (VTEC)

-        Notifiable disease (as are all cases of diarrhoea thought to be due to food poisoning). Contact public health

-        Transmitted via meat or contaminated water

-        O157 causes haemorrhagic colitis in children

·        Profuse watery diarrhoea that turns bloody after 1-3 days

·        Can be afebrile

·        Culture stool, if O157 à admit

·        U+Es, FBC (haemolysis, thrombocytopaenia)

-        Adults with O157 are usu asymptomatic

-        Only a small percentage (~10%) of cases progress to HUS

-        HUS occurs 5-14 days after onset of diarrhoea.

-        Spread can be via faecal-oral route so children must stay off nursery/school until 2 negative stool cultures

 

-        HUS is charterised by

·        Microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia (therefore coombs negative!)

·        Thrombocytopaenia

·        Acute renal failure

 

-        Verotoxin (aka Shiga toxin) is released into the circulation during infection. The toxin then binds to endothelial receptors throughout the body (esp renal, GI and CNS)

-        RBCs are damaged as they pass through partially occluded vessels resulting in haemolysis

-        Toxin release occurs early in infection hence why antibiotics offer no benefit

-        Cases of HUS have been reported in response to other bacteria (Strep pneumoniae) and even in response to some medications.

-        Rx: supportive, avoid anti-diarrhoeals, dialysis if necessary

-        Antibiotics may increase risk of HUS in E. coli O157 infection (controversial).
