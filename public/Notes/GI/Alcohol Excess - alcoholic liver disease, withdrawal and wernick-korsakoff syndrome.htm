Alcoholic Excess

 

-        Safe levels of drinking:

·        Men <3-4 units in one day

·        Women <2-3 units in one day

·        With at least 2 days each week where no alcohol is consumed!

-        this replaces the old recommendation of men: 21 units/week, women 14 units/week

-        Around  a quarter of the UK population regularly consume more than alcohol than the safe level

-        About a half of UK adults admit to binge drinking

 

Alcohol Metabolism

 

-        Ethanol is oxidised to acetylaldehyde by alcohol dehydrogenase in the liver.

-        This is then converted to Acetate in the liver mitochondria

-        Acetate is released into the circulation and is metabolised by peripheral tissues → CO2, fatty acids and water

 

-        An alternative route of alcohol metabolism occurs when excess quantities are taken. Instead of the cytoplasmic enzyme alcohol dehydrogenase, ethanol is converted to acetylaldehyde by Microsomal Enzyme Oxidising System (MEOS) (includes the cytochrome P450 enzymes) which are induced by ethanol consumption.

 

 

 

Consequences of Excess Alcohol Consumption:

 

1)     Alcohol Dependence Syndrome

 

-        physical dependence on or addition to alcohol

-        Tolerance develops

-        Withdrawal symptoms occur on abstinence

-        Compulsive drug-taking behaviour occurs

-        Continue to consume alcohol despite significant substance-related harm

-        Treatment involves psychological, medical and social therapy

Alcoholic Liver Disease

 

Pathology

 

The term alcoholic liver disease encompasses a spectrum of pathology that can result from alcohol excess.

 

Fatty change → Alcoholic Hepatitis → Alcoholic Cirrhosis

 

Only 10-20% of heavy drinkers will develop cirrhosis. It is unknown why some drinkers are susceptible to cirrhosis and others are somewhat protected.

 

Fatty Change

 

-      invariably occurs with alcohol excess

-      changes are reversible on stopping drinking

-      present to minimal degree even after moderate quantities of alcohol

-      mainly in zone 3 of liver acinus

-      Metabolism of alcohol increases NADH/NAD ratio – decreases redox potential → increased hepatic fatty acid synthesis + decreased fatty acid oxidation → accumulation of fatty acids which are esterified to TGs.

-      cells become swollen with fat

-      steatosis can also occur in obesity, diabetes, starvation and chronic illness

-      can lead to fibrosis if collagen is laid down around the central hepatic veins. This can eventually escalate to cirrhosis, even without a preceding hepatitis. Occurs due to the direct effects of alcohol on stellate cells, transforming them into collagen-producing myofibroblast cells.

 

Clinical features of Fatty Changes:

 

-      Often asymptomatic

-      Mild elevation in LFTs

-      Vague abdominal symptoms eg nausea, D+V, are due to direct effects of alcohol on GI system

-      Hepatomegaly can be present

 

 

Alcoholic Hepatitis

 

-      Fatty change + infiltration by WBCs (mainly neutrophils) ie Inflammation

-      Hepatocyte necrosis occurs in zone 3

-      Mallory bodies (dense cytoplasmic inclusions) can be seen in hepatocytes (also seen in Wilson’s and PBC)

-      Can progress to cirrhosis

 

Clinical features of Alcoholic Hepatitis

 

Varying clinical presentation:

-      Patient can be well – hepatitis only present on liver biopsy along with fatty changes

-      Moderate illness – jaundice plus other symptoms of chronic liver. LFTs are deranged. Diagnosed clinically on symptoms + blood tests (or on liver histology).

-      Severe illness – can occur on top of underlying cirrhosis. Fever (due to liver necrosis), pain, jaundice, ascites, hepatomegaly, coagulopathy plus any other signs of chronic liver disease may be present. In severe alcohol hepatitis patients can have changes of decompensated liver disease even if there is no cirrhosis present

 

 

Alcoholic Cirrhosis

 

-      Chronic injury to liver leads to activation of stellate cells. These secrete collagen in the Space of Disse leading to fibrosis. The collagen blocks the sinusoidal fenestrations thus decreasing liver function.

-      Amidst the fibrosis, the hepatocytes continue to regeneration in a classically ‘micronodular’ pattern

 

Clinical features of Alcoholic Cirrhosis

 

-      Can be compensated or decompensated

-      If compensated, the residual viable tissue is sufficient to carry out function and so very few s/s are present

-      Decompensated = ascites, encephalopathy, variceal bleeding, coagulopathy, jaundice

 

 

 

Investigations:

 

-        Bloods

·        LFTs - ↑AST and ALT. ↓albumin (implies decreased synthetic function of liver)

·        GGT - ↑ if recent drinking

·        FBC - ↓Hb + ↑MCV (macrocytic anaemia due to B12 and folate deficiency), ↑WCC (in hepatitis), ↓platelets

·        Coag - ↑PT

 

-        Ultrasound

·        Will show fatty change, ascites, cirrhosis, hepatomegaly or shrunken liver if cirrhosis. Also screens for HCC (However if ↑ AFP it is necessary to do a more sensitive scan eg CT or MRI).

-        Ascitic tap

·        For biochemistry, microbiology and cytology

 

-        CT/MRI

·        Useful if ?HCC, collateral varices.

 

-        Endoscopy – for detection and treatment of varices

 

-        Liver Biospy – not routinely done in alcoholic liver disease but can confirm severity and type of liver disease. Stain for iron and copper if any doubt as to aetiology. Need to make sure coag is OK prior to biopsy.

 

 

Management and Prognosis

 

-        Stop drinking

-        Treat Withdrawal/Delerium Tremens (see below)

-        IV thiamine to prevent Wernicke-korsakoff syndrome (then oral thiamine)

-        Bed rest

-        High protein diet with supplementary vitamins (this is due to high rate of malnutrition amongst liver disease patients). If encephalopathic, the patient may not be able to tolerate high protein – give what they can manage.

-        Monitor coagulation

-        Corticosteroids should be given depending on GAHS.

·        But NOT if co-existing infection is a contraindication!

·        Invasive procedure that bypasses blood from the portal vein straight into the hepatic vein missing out the liver

·        Catheter is inserted in the jugular vein à SVC à IVC à Hepatic vein. Then make a small puncture in Portal vein and place a metal stent to decrease portal pressure

-        Liver transplant is the only cure for cirrhosis

·        Donor livers are very scarce; most are cadaveric.

·        Need to have stopped drinking ≥ 6 months to even be considered for transplant.

·        Require lifelong immunosuppression post-transplant ( eg ciclosporin; tacrolimus)    

·        5 year post-transplant survival ~70-85%

 

-        Without transplant survival is poor if patient continues to drink

·        ~35% survival at 5 years if complications present

 

Alcohol Withdrawal

 

-        Occurs 12-48 hours after last drink

-        Likely in anyone who regularly consumes more than 5 units of alcohol/day for >2 weeks

-        Also in people with known alcohol dependence or alcohol related complications

 

s/s:

-        Mild withdrawal: tremor, nausea, agitation and headache

-        Delirium Tremens is the most serious withdrawal state and can be characterised by disorientation, agitation, anxiety, sweating, hallucinations, and pyrexia.

 

Decision to treat is based on…

 

CIWA score - Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment (Alcohol, Revised)

 

-        67 point score based on 10 common s/s of withdrawal (nausea, sweating, tremor, anxiety, agitation, tactile disturbance, auditory disturbance, visual disturbance, headache, orientation)

-        Performed by trained nurses

 

-        <10 = does not require treatment

-        10-20 = decision to treat is based on clinical judgement. Also if score is rising

-        >20 = treat! “Diazepam as per CIWA”

 

Treatment is with 20mg Diazepam.

 

1-2mg Lorazepam, which has a shorter half-life can be used for elderly patients, patients with decompensated liver disease who will take longer to clear the drug and patients with respiratory disease.

 

Note: some hospitals have recently replaced CIWA with a similar score called the Glasgow Modified Alcohol Withdrawal Score (GMAWS).

 

Managing patients with Alcohol withdrawal as an FY1

 

-        Any patient with a history of alcohol withdrawal symptoms or who regularly consumes >5units/day for >2 weeks should be treated for alcohol withdrawal

 

1)     Pabrinex

 

-        Give immediately to prevent wernicke’s encephalopathy

·        2 pairs of vials IV for 3 days if signs of encephalopathy (confusion, ophthalmoplegia (nystagmus), ataxia)

·        1 pair of vials IV for 3 days if high risk of encephalopathy (malnourished, D+V, dehydrated)

·        Oral thiamine if low risk (alcohol dependant but not other risk factors)

 

2)     Diazepam per CIWA

 

-        To prevent DTs

-        Prescribe on kardex: Diazepam, “as per CIWA”

-        Fill in CIWA chart – Dose = 20mg

-        Nurses then calculate the patient’s CIWA score and administer diazepam appropriately.

 

 

Notes:

-        Cannot start treatment if patient is still intoxicated with alcohol

-        Other CNS depressant drugs (eg opiates) will have an additive effect

-        Diazepam, after total dose 120mg - re-evaluate treatment plan

-        Diazepam has a long half-life (~30 hours) and its metabolites can have an even longer half-life of up to 200 hours, therefore it will exhibit automatic tapering.

-        Lorazepam has a shorter half-life than diazepam and patients receiving this will need their dose tapered over a week

 

 

Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome

 

-        Spectrum of disease resulting from thiamine deficiency.

-        Thiamine is a co-enzyme involved in glucose and lipid metabolism. It is needed to synthesise myelin.

-        Thiamine deficiency results in alcohol excess due to:

·        Alcohol impairs thiamine absorption

·        Malnutrition and protracted vomiting are common with alcohol excess)

·        Also the liver stores B vitamins so cirrhosis decreases reserve stores.

 

-        Occurs in ~2% of alcohol abusers

-        Diagnosis is clinical

-        CT head to rule out other brain pathology

-        Can be difficult to distinguish from alcoholic dementia, hepatic encephalopathy, DTs etc.

 

Wernicke’s encephalopathy:

 

-       Acute onset of neurological symptoms

-       Classical triad in 10%:             Confusion

Ataxia

                                                            Nystagmus

-       Other neurological symptoms can be present

-       Potentially life-threatening.

 

Korsakoff’s Syndrome:

 

-       chronic psychosis

-       Late manifestation resulting from undertreated Wernicke’s

-       Chronic deficiency of thiamine damages neural tissue

-       Characterised by:        Amnesia (anterograde and retrograde)

Confabulation – falsification of memories

Apathy – pt is generally indifferent

 

-       In most extreme cases can result in coma. Mortality ~10-20%

-       Prognosis depends on extent of disease and time until treatment

-       Most patients have some residual deficit: nystagmus (60%), ataxia (60%), amnesia (80%)
