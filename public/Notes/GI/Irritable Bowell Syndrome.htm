Irritable Bowell Syndrome

 

-        functional bowel disease - no organic cause is found

 

-        symptoms are chronic and include:  

§  Abdominal pain

§  Bloating

§  Change in bowel habit

 

-        symptoms often relieved by defecation

-        bowel symptoms vary

·        IBS-D = Diarrhoea

·        IBS-C = Constipation

·        IBS-A = Alternates

 

Pathophysiology:

 

-        Unknown cause – thought to be due to abnormality in the brain-gut axis, ie hypersensitive gut response to psychological stimuli.

-        Other theories include abnormalities in gut flora or gut immune system.

-        IBS does not progress onto other conditions.

-        IBS often occurs in patients with other symptoms including lethargy, nausea, backache, headache, and bladder symptoms and these may support diagnosis.

 

Red-flag Symptoms

 

Investigate further and refer if necessary any:

 

Ø  Unexplained weight-loss

Ø  PR bleeding

Ø  FH of bowel or ovarian cancer

Ø  Age >60 (esp if change in bowel habit for >6 weeks)

Ø  Anaemia

Ø  Rectal/abdominal masses

 

 

 

Investigations

 

-        GI and Rectal Examination

-        FBC

-        CRP

-        Screen for coeliac’s (anti-tissue transglutaminase antibody)

 

-        If these tests are negative, there are no red flags, and history is consistent with IBS it is NOT necessary to investigate further.

 

-        Clinical Alogirthms, such as Rome II can be used to help make a positive diagnosis of IBS however commonly in practice it is simply a diagnosis of exclusion.

 

Management

 

-        General lifestyle guidance – exercise, stress management, diet and symptom-targeted management.

 

-        Review fibre intake

-        Prescribe laxatives or anti-motility agents (loperamide) as required and get patient to adjust dose depending on symptoms/stool consistency.

 

-        If symptoms are still not controlled consider 2nd line therapy

·        low dose Tri-cyclic Antidepressants (used for their analgesic effects)

·        if no response  - try Selective Serotonin Re-uptake Inhibitors

 

-        For people whose symptoms do not respond to pharmacological treatments after 12 months consider referring for:

·        cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT)

·        hypnotherapy

·        psychological therapy.

 

Differential Diagnosis

 

-        Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)

·        Crohn’s disease

·        Ulcerative colitis

-        Coeliac’s disease

-        Infection (eg giardiasis parasite)

-        Hypersensitivity (eg egg)

-        Lactose intolerance (alactasia – absence of lactase enzyme)

-        Radiation colitis (posr-radiotherapy)

 

 

 

Differentiating IBS from IBD

 

The following features support the diagnosis of IBS:

 

-        Pain is relieved by defaecation

-        No blood/mucus

-        No nocturnal symptoms

-        Absence of signs of auto-immune disease (uveitis, erythema nodusum, joint pain, finger clubbing)

-        No clinical or laboratory findings.

 

 

 

 

 

It is important to remember that just because we cannot find a medical cause for the symptoms in patients with IBS, it does not mean that their symptoms do not exist – the patient is genuinely running to the toilet dozens of times a day! For some patients it can be worth exploring the possible role of psychological triggers to their symptoms, however again it is crucial to convey that just because their symptoms may be coming from their head, you are NOT saying they are all in their head! If a patient thinks you have accused them of making it up or being unsympathetic, then you will have damaged that doctor-patient relationship beyond repair. If appropriate, reassure the patient that nothing serious is going on (patients often fear the worst!) then help them identify triggers in their life (stress, work, relationships) that they can address. After all treating the cause is always better than simply chasing symptoms.
