ECG Notes

Conditions you should be able to detect on ECG:

 

*in undergraduate exams, the ECG station is nearly always ST elevation MI (STEMI) or Atrial fibrillation. After all common things are common. However in real life, you sometimes come across these too, so you’ll need to learn to recognise these rhythms at some point too…

 

-        STEMI

-        ST depression

-        Sinus Arrest – ie missed sinus beat

-        Atrial Ectopic beats

-        Ventricular Ectopic beats

-        Sinus Bradycardia

-        Sinus tachycardia

-        Heart Blocks (1st, 2nd, 3rd degree)

-        Bundle Branch Blocks

-        LBBB (William V1,V6)

-        RBBB (Marrow V1, V6)

-        Supra-ventricular Tachycardia

-        Atrial Fibrillation

-        Atrial Flutter

-        Ventricular Tachycardia

-        Ventricular Fibrillation

-        LV Hypertrophy (S-V1 + R-V5 = ≥ 35mm (+ST changes (reverse tick) = strain)

-        Cor pulmonale – peaked p waves

-        Wolf-parkinson White Syndrome (short PR, delta waves, then SVT)

-        Bigeminy; trigeminy – normal beat followed by an ectopic so that you get ‘twinned beats’

-        Torsades du pointes (Polymorphic Ventricular tahcycardia after Long QT syndrome)

-        Hyperkalaemia – ‘low flat p-waves, broad bizarre QRS, slurring into the ST segment, tall tented T-waves’ Dr. Clarke, www.askdoctorclarke.com

-        Hypokalaemia

-        Pulmonary embolism – s1, q3, t3 – S wave in 1, path Q in 3, inverted T in 3

-        Right Axis Deviation – I –ve, current is flowing LAàRA in lead I. Can be normal.

-        Left Axis deviation – I+ve, II-ve ‘one up, two down’

·        To remember which way leads I and II point in axis deviation just remember: in Right axis deviation they are ‘Right for each other’ (the QRS in I and II point together), whereas in left axis deviation they have ‘Left each other’ (QRS in I and II point away)

-        Short QT – hypercalcaemia, hypermagnesia, digoxin, thyrotoxicosis

-        Short PR – accessory pathway eg WPW

-        Digoxin – ‘reverse tick’

-        p-mitrale – bifid p-wave; looks like an ‘m’; suggests left atrial hypertrophy

 

Normal intervals and ranges

 

PR interval = 0.12-0.2s           (3-5 small boxes)

QRS = <0.12s                         (<3 small boxes) (if >0.02s = BBB)

QT – 0.4-0.45s                        (10-15 small boxes)

 

Pathological criteria:

 

ST elevation:

·        2 small boxes in chest lead

·        1 small box in limb leads

 

ECG leads and the heart:

 

Anterior leads = V1-V6

Inferior leads = II, III, aVF

Lateral leads = I, aVL, V6

 

Calculating rate

 

300/no of small boxes

If arrhythmia – [count total number of QRSs in the rhythm strip (10s)] x6

 

 

NB. P waves are most obvious in leads II and V1. so look at these leads to exclude AF.
