Valvular Heart Disease (& Murmurs)

Medical students often get very pre-occupied on hearing murmurs, especially in the run-up to exams. At an undergraduate level all that really matters is whether you can hear a murmur or not – don’t get too stressed about what type of murmur it is (leave that for the echo report because often cardiologists cannot tell for sure!)

 

Even in real hospital life as a junior doctor the times that it is most important to detect a murmur is in patients with history of collapse (?aortic stenosis) and patients with fever (?endocarditis). In both these situations, just being able to detect if a murmur is present (even if you do not know exactly what type it is) is sufficient, because you are going to request further investigations anyway.

 

Systolic Murmurs

 

Ø  Aortic Stenosis

Ø  Mitral Regurgitation

 

-        Much more common and easier to hear

-        They are high pressure so RADIATE in their direction of flow

 

Diastolic Murmurs

 

Nb: DARMS – diastolic = AR + MS

 

Ø  Aortic regurgitation

Ø  Mitral stenosis

 

-        These are difficult to hear, hence you perform the manoeuvres to elicit them

 

 

Murmurs in Exams

 

In exams, if it is a real patient, IT WILL BE A SYSTOLIC MUMUR - common things are common (even in exams).

 

Be careful when reporting the murmur to start general and then narrow down your diagnosis, making sure to explain your reasoning. It’s just like maths at school – show your working!

 

So start by saying…

 

“On auscultation of this patient’s heart a murmur was audible” – this is enough to pass!

 

Then decide if it is systolic or diastolic. You can do this by timing the murmur with the pulse. If the murmur occurs at the same time as you feel the patient’s pulse then it is systolic.

 

“The murmur occurs simultaneously with the pulse and is therefore a systolic murmur” – distinction territory

 

Now you need to decide is it AS or MR.

 

“The murmur is loudest in the aortic area, it radiates to the carotids and is ejection systolic in nature, these findings are consistent with Aortic Stenosis” - now you’re just showing off!

OR

 

“The murmur is loudest in the mitral region, it radiates to the axilla and is pan-systolic in nature, these findings are consistent with Mitral regurgitation”

 

Now cover your ass…

 

“I would want to confirm this diagnosis and assess severity by arranging an ECHO”. Now it doesn’t even matter if you got the murmur wrong!

 

 

 

Aortic stenosis

 

Signs:

-        Ejection systolic murmur

-        Slow rising pulse

-        Loudest in aortic area (direction of flow)

-        Radiates to carotids

-        Forceful non-displaced apex beat ‘heaving’

-        Low volume pulse

-        Narrow pulse pressure on BP

-        Systolic thrill in aortic area

 

Symptoms

 

Outflow obstruction à hypertrophy. Results in…

 

-        Angina – hypertrophy increases O2 demand; cannot increase output during exertion; increased diastolic pressure à decrease coronary blood flow

 

-        Exertional syncope – unable to increase CO + exercise mediated muscular vasodilatation à hypotension à syncope

 

-        HF – contractile dysfunction in hypertrophied heart due to very high afterload. LV Pressure backs up à pulmonary venous hypertension à pulmonary oedema à dyspnoea

 

These all occur late. But consider AS in any elderly patient that presents with angina, syncope or HF. Most AS is asymptomatic.

 

Causes:

 

-        Senile calcification (most common)

·        Fusion of valve cusps, fibrosis and calcification

·        Due to age related degeneration

·        Similar process to atherosclerosis à fibrosis

·        Lipoproteins within the lesions calcify

 

-        Rheumatic Heart disease.

·        Childhood Strep pyogenes causing valve disease in adult life

·        Can cause both aortic stenosis and regurgitation

 

-        Congenital Aortic stenosis

 

Note:

-        Bicuspid valve

o   Increases susceptibility to degenerative change, so presents earlier eg 60s instead of 80s

 

Investigations:

 

-        CXR – often normal; may show calcification.

-        ECG – signs of LVH; p-mitrale (bifid p-wave); LAD (left anterior hemiblock); LBB or complete AV block (due to calcified ring affecting AV node)

-        Echo

·        Shows thickened calcified immobile valve cusps

·        LVH - dimensions and function

·        Measure aortic valve area

·        Doppler can be used to measure the velocity of the jet and from that the pressure gradient across the valve can be calculated. This is used to assess severity ?surgery

-        Cardiac catheterisation

·        Measures dimensions of the valve

·        Also allows measurement of the pressure across the valve.

·        Also used to assess CAD – fit for surgery? Simultaneous CABG repair during the operation?

 

Normally there is very little pressure across the valve – only a few mmHg

<25mmHg = mild aortic stenosis

>50mmHg is severe stenosis and needs surgery

 

Management:

 

-        Valve replacement

·        Aim is to replace before HF occurs

-        Decision when to operate is guided by symptoms and severity

·        All symptomatic patients

·        Gradient across the valve

 

-        Percutaneous valvuplasty can be attempted if the patient is not fit for surgery

-        NB: Don’t give ACEi or nitrates à syncope

 

Aortic Sclerosis

 

-        Is a precursor of aortic stenosis

-        The valve leaflets are thickened and there is a murmur but the pressures are normal

-        The murmur does NOT radiate to the neck

-        The pulse is normal (character and volume)

-        It is more common than full aortic stenosis in which there is narrowing of the valve orifice and obstruction to flow.

 

 

 

Aortic Valve Surgery

 

Indications for surgery

 

-        All symptomatic patients

-        Severe disease on echo

-        Treat before irreversible HF occurs

-        If asymptomatic and early disease – monitor regularly

 

Types of Heart valve

 

Tissue

-        Porcine

-        Shorter lifespan – valves calcify and degenerate quicker

-        ~10-12 years

-        Given to elderly

-        Also patients contraindicated to anticoagulation eg young women of child bearing age

 

Synthetic valve

-        Diff types eg tilting disc; ball and cage

-        Last longer

-        >30 years

-        Require lifelong ANTICOAGULATION!

 

 

 

Mitral regurgitation

 

Signs:

-        Pansystolic murmur at apex

·        Radiates to the axilla

·        Second heart sound not heard separately “burr  |  burr” [1]

-        Displaced apex beat

-        AF

-        RV haeve

-        Quiet first heart sound

 

Causes:

 

-        Functional (LV dilatation)

-        Calcification

-        Rheumatic fever

-        Mitral valve prolapse

-        Rupture chordate tendinae/papillary muscle

-        Post-endocarditis

-        CT disease (eg marfans)

 

Investigations

 

-        ECG – AF; p-mitrale (bifid p-wave; atrial hypertrophy) if not in AF

-        CXR – cardiomegaly; prominent LA; valve calcification

-        Echo

 

Management

 

-        AF – control rate + anticoagulate

-        Diuretics improve symptoms

-        Surgery – aim to REPAIR valve

-        Replacement is also possible

-        Do before LV irreversibly impaired

 

Aortic Regurgitation

 

Signs:

 

-        Early diastolic murmur

·        follows second heart sound

·        “Lub taaarr” [1]

·        Manoeuvre: listen at LSE with patient sitting upright, breath held in forced expiration.

-        Collapsing pulse

-        Corrigan’s sign - collapsing pulse visible in the neck

-        Displaced apex beat

-        Wide pulse pressure

-        JVP not raised

-        Pistol-shot femorals

 

Symptoms:

 

-        Also asymptomatic if early

-        Later stage = exertional dyspnoea

-        Also orthopnoea, PND, angina, HF

-        Can occur acutely in infective endocarditis or ascending aortic dissection(can cause acute HF)

-        If chronic à dilatation

 

Causes:

 

-        Rheumatic heart disease

-        Post-Endocarditis

-        Syphilitic heart disease – small vessel vasculitis affects endocardium of valve

-        Connective tissue disease eg marfans, ehlners-dahnlos

-        Seronegative arthritides

 

Investigations

 

-        CXR - cardiomegaly; calcific valve

-        ECG

-        Echo – dilated LV, aortic root may be enlarged; regurg jet visible on Doppler

-        Cardiac catheterisation – assess severity; CAD

 

Management

 

-        Treat systolic hypertension

-        Echo every 6-12 months to monitor

-        Aim to operate before significant LV dysfunction

 

Mitral Stenosis

 

Signs:

 

-        Mid diastolic murmur “lub de derr” [1]

·        Rumbling low pitched

-        Loud first heart sound – high LA pressure keeps valve open til late in diastole then snaps shut

-        Opening snap – high pitched sound just after S2

-        Apex beat tapping in quality

-        Apex beat not displaced

-        Malar flush – due to ↓CO

-        AF (80%)

-        Palpable first heart sound

 

Causes:

 

-        Rheumatic

-        Congenital

 

Presentation: dyspnoea, fatigue, palpitations, chest x=ray

 

Investigations

 

-        ECG – AF; p-mitrale (bifid p-wave; atrial hypertrophy) if not in AF. RVH, RAD

-        CXR – cardiomegaly; prominent LA; valve calcification

-        Echo

 

Management

 

-        Control AF rate + anticoagulate

-        Diuretics for HF

-        Percutaneous balloon valvuloplasty – to open valve

-        If fails, open mitral valve replacement

 

 

3rd Heart sound

 

-        Normal in children and young adults <40.

-        If occurs >40 = pathological

-        Occurs due to blood rushing into the ventricle

-        “lub de-dub” -  a gallop rhythm [1]

-        Causes:

o   Heart failure

o   MI

o   Cardiomyopathy

o   Hypertension

o   Mitral and aortic regurg

o   Constrictive pericarditis

 

4th heart sounds

 

-        Occurs due to atrial contraction into a non-compliant or hypertrophied ventricle

-        Always abnormal

-        “le-lub dub” [1]

-        Causes:

o   Heart failure

o   MI

o   Cardiomyopathy

o   Hypertension

 

 

Random note

If a patient is having palpitations – are they only at rest? Ectopics stop on exertion due to decreased diastole à decreases opportunity for ectopic beats. If palpitations occur on exertion = more worrying

 

Grading Murmurs

 

-        Murmurs can be graded 1-6 depending on how loud they are

-        Does not necessarily correlate with severity

·        because a large hole à less turbulence à quiet murmur

-        Murmurs 4+ are associated with a thrill

-        A grade 3 murmur is most likely in OSCE exam

 

1

Very faint; only heard by experts in optimal conditions

2

Soft

3

Loud

4

Loud + thrill

5

Very loud (audible with stethoscope partly off chest)

6

Audible without a stethoscope + thrill

 

Right-heart valve disease

-        much less common

-        often associated with intravenous drug use

 

Tricuspid Regurgitation

 

-        most common right-heart valvular lesion

-        pansystolic murmur

·        difficult to hear

·        loudest at left sternal edge

-        ↑↑JVP

·        giant v-waves à appears monophasic (ie looks more like a pulse than the normal double-flutter of the JVP)

-        tender PULSATILE hepatomegaly
