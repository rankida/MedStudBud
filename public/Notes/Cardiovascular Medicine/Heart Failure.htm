Heart failure

-        Heart failure is a syndrome, not a diagnosis

-        It describes the s/s resulting from inadequate cardiac output

-        Poor prognosis: 50% die in 5 years

 

Triad of symptoms:

 

Ø  Dyspnoea

Ø  Fatigue

Ø  Oedema

 

 

Causes:

 

-        IHD, HTN, valvular heart disease, arrhythmias, cardiomyopathies, alcohol, congenital heart disease

Signs

 

-        ↑HR, cold peripheries, cyanosis, low BP, narrow pulse pressure on BP, displaced apex (dilatation), left parasternal haeve (= RV hypertrophy 2º pulmonary hypertension), murmurs, exhaustion.

 

Investigations

 

Diagnosis requires s/s of HF + objective evidence of cardiac dysfunction

 

1st line

Ø  ECG

Ø  BNP

à If both are normal = not HF (NICE)

à If abnormal à do ECHO!

 

Echo

-        Gold standard!

-        Shows abnormal wall contraction - ?MI

-        Valve dysfunction

-        Dilatation or hypertrophy

-        LV dysfunction – ejection fraction?

 

Other tests:

 

-        FBC – anaemia

-        U+Es – monitor K – drugs can alter it; disturbances can precipitate arrhythmias

-        Also: TFTs, LFTs, Ferritin (IDA? haemochromatosis?)

-        ABGs

-        Troponin if acute setting (± chest pain)

 

-        CXR

·        Cardiomegaly

·        Upper lobe venous diversion (prominent upper lobe veins)

·        Interstitial oedema

§  Kerley-B lines

§  Prominent fissures

§  Peri-bronchial cuffing – interstitial fluid accumulates around bronchi; appear like donoughts if seen end on

§  Entire lung field affected

·        Pleural effusions (often bilateral)

·        Alveolar shadowing – ‘Bat’s wing appearance’ – peri-hilar shadowing with relative sparing of the peripheries

·         

-        ECG – may show cause; rare to have a completely normal ECG in HF

 

-        BNP – 97% negative predictive value; also prognostic value.

 

 

Management of Acute Heart Failure

 

Acute HF = medical emergency

 

-        Sit patient upright

-        100% O2 (not if COPD)

-        IV access  - avoid fluids!!!

-        Monitor ECG

-        Treat underlying causes:

o   PCI if MI; cardiovert if AF; shock if VT/VF; pericardiocentesis for tamponade any arrhythmias; pacing is bradycardia.

-        IV Opiates (Diamorhpine 2.5-5mg) à Pulmonary vasodilator, anxiolytic, venodilator so decreases preload. Caution if respiratory failure/COPD (resp depressant)

-        Furosemide 40-80mg IV

-        GTN spray – not if BP <90

-        If BP>100 nitrate infusions – IV isorbide dinitrate

-        If still breathless à CPAP!

 

If BP <90 = cardiogenic shock

 

-        Get help immediately (ICU/CCU)

-        Inotropes eg dobutamine

-        Consider intra-aortic balloon pump!

 

Acute right ventricular failure

 

-        Can suspect on ECG if inferior MI but confirm on echo

-        Usu due to inferior MI (occlusion of right coronary artery)

-        This is the only case in which you would give fluids!

-        And avoid drugs that decrease filling of the ventricle ie diuretics and nitrates

-        Should be done by experts on CCU.

 

 

Fluids in chest pain? You do NOT give fluids to patients with cardiac chest pain/ACS. If they are having a large infarct it could precipitate acute HF. Also they usu have a short history of symptoms and so are not fluid depleted. If hypotensive treat any arrhythmias and treat as cardiogenic shock (get an echo to rule out right infarct but otherwise do not give fluids!)

 

Management of chronic HF

 

General factors:

-        Stop smoking

-        Decrease salt intake and fluid restrict (1.5l)

-        Moderate exercise

-        Avoid alcohol

-        Optimise weight and nutrition

-        Vaccinations – flu and pneumococcal

-        Treat cause eg valve disease, arrhythmia

-        Treat exacerbating factors eg anaemia, thyrotoxicosis, stop NSAIDs, verapamil (-ve inotrope).

 

Drugs:

-        Furosemide – loop diuretic; initially works as a venodilator to decrease preload à early symptom relief. Diuresis is a secondary effect à ↓IV volume.

-        ACEi – improves symptoms and prolongs life. Give to ALL patients with HF; If cough give ARB

-        Spironolactone – aldosterone antagonist; K-sparing diuretic. Give to NYHA III-IV.

-        Beta blockers – eg carvedilol; bisoprolol; decrease myocardial O2 demand and increase ventricular filling; inhibit ↑sympathetic activity seen in HF; decrease mortality in HF; start low and go slow. Give to ALL patients but NOT in acute setting – can be fatal!

-        Nitrates – venodilators; decrease preload

-        Digoxin – esp if AF

-        Vasodilators – hydralazine or nitrates (isorbide dinitrate) are options, esp if cannot take ACEi/ARB

 

Drugs to avoid in HF:

 

-        CCBs = -vely inotropic

-        Antiarrthymics (except amiodarone) = -vely inotropic

-        NSAIDs – affect renal function; can ↑ fluid retention

-        Steroids - can ↑ fluid retention

 

 

Advanced Treatments

 

Ø  Biventricular pacing/cardiac resynchronisation therapy (CRT)

Ø  Implantable Cardiac Defibrillator (ICD)

Ø  Ventricular assist device (VAD)

Ø  Cardiac transplant

 

Biventricular pacing/CRT

 

-        Aka cardiac resynchronisation therapy

-        Pacing leads placed in RA, RV and LV (via coronary sinus)

-        Most useful in patients with evidence of abnormal intraventricular conduction ie LBBB, in which the slow cell-to-cell conduction seen in LBBB results in asynchronous contraction of the ventricles

-        Improves symptoms and prognosis

 

Implantable cardioverter defibrillator

 

-        Machine detects tachyarrhythmia

-        If VT or VF à delivers a shock

-        Improves survival

-        …but not symptoms

 

Can combine CRT and ICD

 

Heart transplant

 

-        Mortality 10-15% at 1 year

-        Mean survival ~10 years

 

Ventricular Assist Devices

 

-        Very expensive

-        Done as a bridge to transplant

-        Mechanical circulatory device that augments the function of the heart

 

The Future?

 

-        Xenotransplant

-        Artificial transplant

-        Stem cells

 

 

 

 

 

NYHA Classification of Chronic HF

 

I – evidence of HF present but no dysnoea on ordinary activities

II – dyspnoea on ordinary activities.

III – dyspnoea on less than ordinary activity. Only comfortable at rest

IV – severe limitation. Dyspnoea at rest

 

Can also think of it in terms of limitation: none (I), mild (II), moderate (II), severe (IV)

 

 

Extra Notes:

Terminology [1]

 

Systolic vs diastolic failure

 

Systolic failure

-        Inability of ventricle to contract normally à ↓ CO

-        Aka LV dysfunction - ejection fraction (EF) (<40%)

-        Causes include IHD (MI), cardiomyopathy

 

Diastolic failure

-        Inability of ventricle to relax à poor filling + ↑filling pressures

-        EF is maintained ie >50% (aka preserved LV function)

-        Causes: restrictive pericarditis, tamponade, hypertension

 

*Systolic and diastolic failure usu co-exist!

 

LHF vs RHF

 

-        Can occur separately or together (= congestive cardiac failure (CCF))

 

LVF

-        s/s: dyspnoea, poor exercise tolerance, fatigue, orthopnoea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea (due to reabsoprtion of peripheral oedema when lying flat overloading intravascular volume), nocturnal cough ± pink frothy sputum, wheeze (cardiac ‘asthma’), nocturia, cold peripheries, weight loss (cahexia – due to decreased gut perfusion and gut oedema à decreased absorption), muscle wasting.

 

RVF

-        Causes: LVF, pulmonary stenosis, lung disease (cor pulmonale)

-        Peripheral oedema (up to waist), raised JVP, ascites, nausea, anorexia, facial engorgement, epistaxis.

 

*the most common cause of right heart failure is left heart failure!

 

Acute vs Chronic HF

 

-        Acute HF = acute pulmonary oedema

·        Either new onset or decompensation of chronic

·        With or without signs of peripheral hypoperfusion

·        Other causes of acute pulmonary oedema eg ARF, ARDS

 

-        Chronic HF is slowly progressive

·        Venous congestion is common but arterial pressure (ie BP) is maintained until late

 

*patients with HF can have ↑, ↓ or normal BP

*If BP <90 à cardiogenic shock!

 

Low output vs High output

 

-        Low – CO falls and fails to increase with exertion

·        Pump failure – can be due to systolic or diastolic HF; bradycardia, -ve inotropes (eg CCBs)

·        Excessive preload (fluid overload eg NSAID, IV fluids running too fast)

·        Excessive afterload – HTN, aortic stenosis

 

-        High output

·        RARE

·        Normal CO or even increased but demand is increased

·        Failure occurs when heart fails to meet demand

·        Can occur in a normal heart but occurs earlier if existing heart disease

·        Causes: anaemia, thyrotoxicosis, pregnancy, paget’s disease

·        à RHF then LHF

 

 

Pathophysiology of HF

 

BP = CO x TPR

 

-        ↓CO à ↓BP detected by baroreceptors.

è ↑sympathetic drive à ↑HR and ↑TPR (vasoconstriction)

è Neurohormonal response – R-A-A system

 

Preload

 

-        ‘the filling pressure of the heart’

-        The pressure exerted on the cardiac myocytes in response to diastolic filling. This tension in the ventricular wall ‘primes’ the heart pump.

 

Afterload

 

-        The resistance against which the heart has to pump

 

Local compensatory changes in myocardium

 

-        Dilatation

o   ↑preload à dilatation

o   Preload increases due to increased venous pressure

o   Right heart output must equal left heart output, therefore if left ventricle is failing (eg due to MI) it cannot cope with the volumes ejected from the right heart. It compensates for this by dilatation.

o   Dilatation à ↑end diastolic volume

o   This initially ↑ contraction due to Starling’s law

o   Dilatation maintains SV but at the expense of increasing filling pressure (preload)

 

-        Hypertrophy

o   Myocytes respond to increased pressure by hypertrophy

o   Initially beneficial because allows increased force of contraction

o   As it continues to grow à outgrows muscle supply à ↓efficiency

o   NB angiotensin II is a cardiac growth factor

 

Starling’s law of the heart

-        As end diastolic volume increases, stroke volume also increases up to a point

-        This is due to the Frank-Starling curve which says that as stretch of a myocyte increases the forces of contraction generated by the myocyte also increases (up to a point).

 

 

Neurohormonal Changes in HF

 

1)     Increased sympathetic activity

 

2)     Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system

 

-        ↓BP à ↓renal perfusion à rennin secreted from juxtaglomerular apparatus

-        Converts angiotensinogen (from liver) to angiotensin I

-        In lungs, ACE converts ang I à ang II

-        Angiotensin II à vasoconstriction

-        Also stimulates adrenal cortex to produce aldosterone

-        Aldosterone ↑Na+H2O re-absorption in kidneys

 

3)     Natriuretic peptides (ANP & BNP)

 

-        One of the few beneficial compensatory mechanisms in HF

-        Released by the atrium (ANP) and ventricle (BNP) in response to stretch

-        Decrease Na +H2O re-absorption in kidney

-        ↑GFR

-        Also decrease preload by relaxing smooth muscle.

-        ANP partly inhibits RAA

 

 

BNP – ‘B-type natriuretic peptide’ – originally called ‘brain’ because it was first isolated from pig brain. Now known to be secreted by the ventricles in response to stretch! BNP levels are closely related to LV pressure. BNP is used in the diagnosis of HF, to distinguish it from other causes of dyspnoea.

 

4)     Increased ADH
