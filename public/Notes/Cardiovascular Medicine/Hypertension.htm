Hypertension

-        Persistently raised BP >140/90

-        Can be systolic, diastolic or both (systolic most important)

-        Measure twice on 2 separate occasions under best conditions possible

-        Complications: CVD, Stroke, PVD, HF (LVH), RF, visual impairment (retinopathy)

Grades of Hypertension

 

  • Mild                 – 140-159/90-99
  • Moderate         – 160-179/100-109
  • Severe             –  >180/110

 

 

 

-        Predominately managed in primary care

-        Screen BP every 3 years if >50

-        Refer if: ?secondary cause

 

 

 

Measuring BP

 

-        Quiet relaxed calm environment

-        Patient seated >5 minutes

-        Can use either arm (if one higher than the other use higher value)

-        Arm outstretched and supported at level of heart

-        Cuff should be able to fit around the arm twice

-        Estimate systolic BP by palpating for disappearance of brachial pulse while inflating the cuff

-        Inflate cuff a further 30mmHg

-        Deflate at 2mmHg /second

o   Systolic = Korotkoff I – appearance of sustained tapping

o   Diastolic = Korotkoff V – disappearance of sounds. However in some patients (eg pregnant women) sounds are present until 0 mmHg. In these patients Korotkoff IV (the muffling of sounds) should be used.

 

-        If >140/90, measure again at end of consultation

o   If still high, repeat in 1-3 months

 

-        If ?postural hypotension – measure their BP while standing (for up to 3 mins)

 

-        Can only use machines is properly calibrated and validated as per manufacturer instructions

-        Routine use of ambulatory BP monitoring is not recommended in primary care

o   Consider if ?white coat hypertension or hypotension in response to treatment

o   Loss of nocturnal dip is a poor prognostic sign

 

 

Threshold for offering Drug treatment:

 

Ø  Persistent BP >160/100

Ø  Isolated systolic hypertension >160

Ø  BP >140/90 in patients with

·        10-year risk of CVD > 20%

·        Signs of target organ damage (incl existing CVD)

 

*CVD risk = risk of stroke and MI

Have lower threshold for treatment in patients with diabetes

Aim: to reduce BP <140/90 (130/80 if diabetic)

 

Signs of end organ damage

 

v  ECG – LVH (S wave in V2 + R wave V6 >35mm)

v  Kidneys proteinuria

v  Retinopathy – fundoscopy

v  PMH of CVD/MI

v  PMH of stroke/TIA

v  PMH of PVD

 

Global CV risk

 

-        Can be calculated using tables in the BNF

-        Calculated using: Diabetes, age, male, smoker, systolic BP, total serum cholesterol:HDL ratio.

-        View the tables at: http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK45860/figure/summary.fu1/?report=objectonly

-        For more information see:

http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/bookshelf/br.fcgi?book=nicecg18&part=summary

 

 

If hypertension without existing CV disease consider formal assessment of CV risk

 

-        Urine dipstick for protein

-        Bloods: plasma glucose, U+Es, serum total cholesterol and HDL

-        ECG

 

Case:

 

52 year old male, non-smoker with persistent BP of 145/95.

 

1)     Signs of end organ damage?

è History

è ECG

è Dip urine

è Fundoscopy

 

2)     Global CVD risk >20%?

è Measure glucose

è Cholesterol

è BP

è Smoking status

 

If none present à Lifestyle advice and monitor BP every 3 months.

 

 

Management

 

Lifestyle interventions

 

è Diet, weight loss, exercise, alcohol, reduce salt, smoking cessation.

 

Pharmacological Management

 

ACD algorithm

 

o   If < 55                               à ACEi (or A2RB)

o   If > 55 (or black)               à CCB or Thiazide diuretic

 

-        If still not controlled, use either A+D or A+C

·        (i.e. dual therapy always involves an ace inhibitor/A2RB)

 

For a picture diagram od the ACD algorithm see: NICE clinical Guideline 34. 2006. Hypertension: Management of Adults in Primary Care. http://www.nice.org.uk/CG034

 

General Notes:

 

-        Aim for BP <140/90 (130/80 of diabetic)

-        *Reduce blood pressure slowly (can precipitate stroke if rapid)

-        Most drugs take 4-6 weeks to have maximum effect!!!

-        Address global CV risk - aspirin and statin?

-        Review hypertension annually.

 

In the UK Guidelines no longer recommend the use of Beta blockers as first line agents for hypertension (due to poorer efficacy and potential increase in risk of new-onset diabetes). Interestingly European guidelines do not distinguish Beta blockers from the other drugs stating they are all equally effective. Also the European guidelines also advocate pharmacological management for all patients with BP >140/90!

 

Extra Notes:

Types of Drugs

 

ALWAYS WARN PATIENT OF POTENTIAL SIDE-EFFECTS PRIOR TO STARTING A NEW DRUG

 

  • Thiazide diuretics

 

-        Eg Bendroflumethiazide  - 2.5mg OD

-        CI: gout

-        S/e: hypokalaemia, hyponatraemia, postural hypotension, impotence.

 

·      Calcium channel blockers

 

-        Eg Amlodipine – 5-10mg OD

-        S/e: flushes, oedema (ankle, face), gum hyperplasia, fatigue

 

  • ACE inhibitors

 

-        Eg  Ramipril – 10mg OD

-        CI: bilatertal renal artery stenosis, aortic valve stenosis

-        s/e:  irritant cough, 1st dose hypotension, hyperkalaemia, can precipitate RF, angioedema

-        ACEi are paradoxically given in Chronic Renal disease

 

  • Angiotensin II Receptor Antagonists (A2RBs)

 

-        Eg Losartan – 50mg OD

-        Caution if valve disease or cardiomyopathy

-        Monitor K

-        s/e: urticaria, vertigo, pruritis, hyperkalaemia

                                             

  • Direct Renin inhibitors

 

-        Eg Aliskiren

-        New drug, role uncertain

-        s/e: diarrhoea, rash, hyperkalaemia (monitor K)

 

  • Beta blockers

 

-        Eg Bisoprolol eg 2.5-5mg OD

-        CI: asthma, acute HF

-        s/e: bronchospasm, cold peripheries, fatigue, impotence, HF.

-        Very useful in patients who also have angina, AF or CHF

-        Also consider in women of child bearing age; increased sympathetic activity.

-        NB when withdrawing a Beta blocker step down dose gradually

 

Types of Hypertension

 

1)     Primary Hypertension

·        Aka Essential or idiopathic

·        >95% of cases

 

2)    Secondary Hypertension (<5%)

·        Hypertension secondary to an identifiable cause

 

Chronic Renal Disease

-        The most common secondary cause

-        Any chronic kidney disease can lead to decreased excretion of Na à fluid retention à increased intravascular volume à increased cardiac output (Frank-starling)

-        Chronic renal disease is usu intrinsic:

·        Primary glomerular disease eg Glomerulonephritis

·        Secondary glomerular disease – eg Diabetes, SLE, RA, systemic sclerosis,

·        Also polycystic kidneys, chronic pyelonephritis

 

Renal Artery Stenosis

-        Atherosclerosis à decreased blood flow to the kidney à increase renin à RAA à vasoconstriction and fluid retention

-        Can also lead to chronic renal failure

 

Endocrine disease

-        Cushing’s (cortisol)

·        Glucocorticoid excess à increased vasoconstriction

·        s/s: central obesity, striae, facial oedema and redness, diabetes, bruising, muscle weakness, depression, oesteoporosis.

·        Can be iatrogenic (long term steroid therapy, adrenal adenoma (uncommon) or pituitary adenoma (↑ACTH)

·        Diagnose by urinary cortisol (24 hour collection), dexamethasone suppression test.

·        Once diagnosed à investigate cause!

 

-        Conn’s syndrome (aldosterone)

·        Adrenal adenoma or carcinoma

·        à primary hyperaldosteronism!

·        ↑Na+, ↓K+  - check U+Es in all patients with high blood pressure

·        Specifically exclude conn’s if: young (<40), low K+, or resistant HTN

 

-        Phaochromacytoma (catecholamines)

·        TRIAD of EPISODIC – headache, sweating and palpitations

·        Also: labile or postural BP, pallor or diaphoresis (ie excessive sweating)

·        suspect in any patient with very variable BP

·        tumour of adrenal medulla à increased catecholamines

·        alpha-adrenoreceptors à vasoconstriction

·        Beta-adrenoreceptors à tachycardia

·        Diagnose by measuring urinary catecholamines (24 hour collection)

·        Majority are benign

·        ~15% are malignant à 50% mortality!

 

-        Hyperthyroidism

·        increased sympathetic drive

-        Hyperparathyroidism

·        Unknown cause

 

Others:

-        Pregnancy, coarctation of the aorta, sleep apnoea, MAOI antidepressants, OCP.

 

If ?Secondary cause

 

è U+Es

è TFTs

è 24hr urine collection

o   Free cortisol (cushings)

o   Catecholamines (phaochromocytoma)

è Renal Ultrasound artery doppler

 

 

Malignant Hypertension

 

-        Severe hypertension >200/130

-        + bilateral retinal haemorrhages

-        +/- papilloedema

-        Patient often has symptoms due to hypertension eg headache, visual disturbance

-        Can precipitate hypertensive emergencies – ARF, encephalopathy, pulmonary oedema (require IV antihypertensives)

-        More common in younger patients

-        Look hard for underlying cause

 

Grading Hypertensive Retinopathy

 

I – silver or copper wiring – tortuous vessels with shiny walls

II – AV nipping – narrowing where arteries cross veins)

III - Flame haemorrhages and cotton wool spots

IV - Papilloedema
