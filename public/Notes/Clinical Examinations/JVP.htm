Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP)

-        Clinical sign used as an indicator of right heart pressure

-        Looking for a pulsation in the internal jugular vein

-        The pulsation may or may not visible in normal subjects

-        The only clinical information that can be gained is whether the JVP is elevated or not

·        Elevated = >4cm above a vertical line drawn from the sterno-manubrial angle of Louis

·        If elevatedà right heart failure.

To examine the JVP:

 

·        Have the patient sitting at 45º, with their neck relaxed (make sure their head is resting on a pillow).

·        Then ask them to look to their left

·        The JVP, if visible, can be seen in the line between the heads of the sternocleidomastoid muscle up and the ear lobe/posterior to the angle of the mandible).

 

 

Differentiating a venous pulsation from an arterial one

 

è i.e is it the JVP or the carotid pulse?

 

Venous pulses should be

-        Non palpable

-        Compressible

-        If compressed it should fill from above

-        JVP has a double waveform

-        Hepatojugular reflux normally produces a transient rise in JVP

·        Note, if it remains elevated during 15s of compression à RVF

 

You can also comment on the waveform of the JVP

 

-        Normally the JVP has a biphasic waveform – ie it has 2 pulsations for every one arterial pulse

-        The first pulsation (a) is due to atrial contraction. The second pulsation occurs at the start of ventricular systole as blood enters the right atrium against a closed tricuspid valve (c)

·        A raised JVP with normal waveform = RVF

·        An absent ‘a’ wave occurs in AF

·        A large a wave occurs in pulmonary hypertension   

·        A raised JVP with absent pulsation occurs in SVC obstruction
