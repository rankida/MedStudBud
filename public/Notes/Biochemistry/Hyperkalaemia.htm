Hyperkalaemia

-        K+ normal values 3.5 – 5.0

·        If >6.5mmol/L =  medical emergency à risk of VF arrest

-        s/s:

·        Cardiac symptoms (palpitations, fast irregular pulse, chest pain), weakness

-        ECG:

·        “low flat p-waves, broad bizarre QRS, slurring into ST segment, tall tented p-waves” Dr Clarke

 

Causes:

 

-        Renal failure (most common) – oliguric, can be acute

-        Metabolic acidosis (eg DKA)

-        Excess IV K+

-        Massive blood transfusion (the older the packs the more cells lysed and therefore the greater the K+ conc)

-        ACE inhibitors

-        Addisons disease

 

Falsely high K+

-        If haemolysed sample, contamination with EDTA from FBC bottle (should do U+Es before FBC!), taken from the ‘drip-arm’, delayed analysis.

 

When called to see a patient with hyperkalaemia:

-        Check fluid chart – excess IV K+?

-        Check kardex – are they on Sando K?

-        Do an ECG!

 

Management:

 

-        If K >5.5 à ECG

-        If K >6 à call senior

 

-        Calcium Gluconate

·        Protects the heart – esp important if ECG changes

·        Give by slow injection over 5 mins with ECG monitoring

·        Must flush venflon before and after

 

-        INSULIN + DEXTROSE

·        Increases intracellular K

·        10units Actrapid made up in 50mls of 50% dextrose

 

-        Nebulised Salbutamol

·        Also drives K into the cells

·        5mg Slabutamol via nebuliser

 

-        Repeat U+Es after 30 minutes. If still high, repeat above.

 

Other treatments to consider:

 

-        Renal Replacement therapy

·        Removes K+. Esp used if renal failure.

·        Consultant decision

 

-        Ion binding resin (eg Calcium resonium)

·        Prevents further absorption of K form the GIT

·        Most commonly used in conditions were the hyperkalaemia is likely to recur (eg CRF)

·        They are cat-ion exchange resins, meaning in the large intestine they swap Ca2+ for K+ to form a complex that cannot be absorbed so is excreted in the faeces

·        Take ~24 hours to have effect

 

ECG changes of Hyperkalaemia

 

-        ECG changes occur sequentially as K+ levels continue to rise. This is because different tissues within the heart are susceptible to varying degrees of hyperkalaemia.

·        Peaked ‘tented’ T waves – earliest sign

·        Prolonged PR interval

·        Flattened or absent P waves

·        Broad QRS = very high risk of arrest
