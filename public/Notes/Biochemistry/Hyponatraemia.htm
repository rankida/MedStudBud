Hyponatraemia

-        = low serum sodium concentration (<135mmol/l; <120mmol/l = severe)

-        Commonest electrolyte abnormality (esp likely if existing renal impairment)

-        Na is normally controlled by thirst, anti-diuretic hormone (ADH) secretion and the renal handling of sodium (aldosterone).

-        Serum sodium concentration depends on the amount of both Na and water in the plasma

-        Therefore hyponatraemia can results from

·        ↑ loss of Na

·        ↑ water (eg oedema)

 

-        Note: it is almost impossible for hyponatraemia to result from too little intake. A modern diet includes about 100x the amount of Na we evolved for!

 

Causes:

 

First of all ask: Is the patient dehydrated?

 

·        à Yes - losing Na+ and water from somewhere

§  Check urinary Na

 

o       If high à Renal loss

·        Eg Renal failure, addisons disease, diuretics (usu thiazides), osmolar (eg hyperglycaemia)

 

o       If normal à GIT

·        Eg vomit, diarrhoea, stoma, fistula.

 

·        à No – Is the patient oedematous?

 

§  Yes =  HF, RF, LF, nephrotic syndrome.

 

§  No

o       Check urine osmolality

·        High = SIADH

·        Low = water overload; severe hypothyroism

Notes:

 

-        False Na values can occur in hyperglycaemia (eg DKA) or if very high lipids or if the blood sample is taken from the drip arm

-        Any sick patient will have a slightly lower Na concentration due to leakiness of capillaries as part of SIRS response.

-        Iatrogenic hyponatraemia occurs if a pt is given too much dextrose (and not enough saline). The dextrose is rapidly metabolised, making the fluid hypotonic and causing ↓Na. This is especially a problem in patients with renal impairment!

 

The most common drugs for causing hyponatraemia are:

 

è Thiazide diuretics

è SSRIs eg fluoxetine

è Omeprazole

 

Note: the loop diuretic furosemide actually has a Na sparing effect in that it increases fluid loss greater than it increases Na loss.

 

Presentation

 

-        Symptoms depends on the level and rate of fall

 

Symptoms

 

-        Mild - anorexia, headache, nausea, vomiting, lethargy

-        Moderate - personality change, muscle cramps and weakness, confusion, ataxia

-        Severe - drowsiness, ↓memory, ↓consciousness, seizures

 

REMEMBER HYPERNATRAEMIA CAN RESULT FROM BOTH DEHYDRATION AND FLUID OVERLOAD, so there may be signs of hypo or hypervolaemia.

 

·        Signs of hypovolaemia - dry mucous membranes, tachycardia, diminished skin turgor

 

·        Signs of hypervolaemia - pulmonary creps, S3 gallop (third heart sound), ↑JVP, peripheral oedema, ascites

 

 

Investigations

 

As an FY1, if a patient has a low Na the investigations you should order are:

 

·        Repeat U+Es – Na, K

·        Plasma osmolality – to check for SIADH

·        Urine osmolality – If high despite, plasma osmolality being low à SIADH

·        Urinary Na – Na being lost via the kidneys?

 

Other investigations to consider:

 

-        CXR – lung ca, CCF?

-        Short synacthen test

-        TFTs – hypothyroid?

 

NB: if there is an obvious cause for the low sodium no further investigation may be necessary.

 

Management

 

-        Treat underlying cause (e.g. stop thiazide).

-        Never base treatment on Na concentration alone – treat the patient in front of you!

-        Ask the nurses for accurate fluid balance monitoring

 

·        If mild hyponatraemia à no treatment.

 

·        If severe or symptomatic…depends on fluid status

 

§  Dehydrated? à give 0.9% saline

§  Fluid overload (most common) à fluid restrict + loop diuretic

§  If normovolaemic (eg SIADH)

o       Fluid restrict

o       Demeclocycline - ADH antagonist, produces partial nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (ie the kidneys do not respond to ADH).

o       Vaptins – vasopressor receptor anytagonists are increasingly being tried.

 

Emergency Hyponatraemia

 

-        Eg seizures, coma

-        Call senior for help!

-        Correct hyponatraemia with 0.9% saline (avoid hypertonic saline).

-        Be wary of HF and Central pontine myelinolysis

 

-        Must correct Sodium slowly – max 0.5mmol/l/hour

·        If increase Sodium too fast → Central Pontine Myelinolysis (Symptoms occur 2-4 days later and are potentially permanent; quadriplegia and pseudobulbar palsy, 'locked in' syndrome).

 

Notes:

-        Whether a patient has symptoms of hyponatraemia or not largely depends on the rate at which their concentration has fallen.

-        Unlike K+, it is total Na content, not serum concentration, that is important. Therefore history is essential!

 

Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH secretion (SIADH)

 

-        An important but uncommon cause of hyponatraemia

-        ADH (vasopressin) is normally secreted in response to low plasma volume or high osmolality

-        Excess ADH results in excessive reabsorption of water from the kidneys. This dilutes the plasma à hyponatraemia

-        Overall the patient is euvolaemic though, because other regulatory systems eg Aldosterone are down regulated.

-        Diagnosis is confirmed by the presence of high urinary Na and urinary osmolality, despite low plasma Na and low plasma osmolality.

·        Ie the patient is inappropriately peeing out lots of Na even though their serum Na is low

 

-        Asymptomatic finding or presents with s/s of hyponatraemia

 

Causes of SAIDH:

 

-        Can be from the pituitary or ectopic sources

-        Malignancy (small cell lung cancer; others), pneumonia, SSRIs, trauma, HIV.

 

Management

 

-        Fluid restrict

-        Consider salt + loop diuretic if severe

-        Demeclocyine is rarely used

-        Vaptins may have a role

 

 

 

Guidelines and further Reading:

 

-        Refer to local protocols

-        Oxford Handbook of Clinical Medicine. 8th edition. 2009. Page 686

Hypernatraemia

-        Much less common that hyponatraemia

-        Usu due to water loss in excess of sodium loss

·        Fluid loss without replacement (can occur in D, V, burns)

·        Diabetes insipidus

·        Primary aldosteronism

·        Excessive IV saline in patients with renal impairment

 

Management

 

-        Give water orally if possible

-        Otherwise give 5% dextrose SLOWLY (to prevent fluid shifts), or can give 0.9% saline if the patient is volume depleted
