General Management of Overdoses

For all overdoses log into TOXBASE (toxbase.com) and print out the appropriate management for the patient. Stick a copy in their notes and follow it.

 

-        ABCs

-        ↓absorption - decontaminate with activated charcoal (if <1 hour)

-        ↑Elimination

-        Find an antedote

-        Manage general complications

-        Refer psychiatry

 

Managing a patient with suspected overdose:

 

General principles:

 

-        Get history if possible (including witnesses)

o   However be aware identifying the toxin from the history is very unreliable – always still do a full Tox-screen!

 

-        A – clear mouth and throat

-        B – O2, ABGs

-        C – IV access, fluids if shocked

-        D – Assess GCS, check glucose

 

-        Call ITU early if concerned (esp if GCS <8)

 

Examine:

-        pupils, heart rate, temperature, signs of other complications (eg CXR - ?aspiration)

 

Monitor

-        ECG, BP, O2 sats, GCS

-        Bloods: U&Es, LFTs, glucose, ABG, clotting, bicarbonate, amylase, FBC (platelets)

 

Supportive

-        Control seizures

-        Treat hypoxia, hypotension, dehydration, hypo/hyperthermia, acidosis

 

Activated Charcoal

-        Large surface area binds toxins and therefore ↓ absorption

-        50g single or repeated dose

-        Does not work for all toxins eg heavy metals, ethanol, acids.

 

Gastric lavage

-        NG tube is passed, saline is infused and then gastric contents are suctioned

-        Fallen out of favour due to punitive connotations. Also risk of perforation.

-        Can only be done if <1 hour. Not if corrosives ingested.

-        Still used in lithium OD.

 

è Send blood and urine for toxicology screening

è ALWAYS measure paracetamol and salicylate levels

è Psychiatric review when medically fit

 

Paracetamol Overdose

-        Risk of liver damage is proportional to the quantity of paracetamol taken

-        Smaller amounts of paracetamol can lead to hepatocellular damage in high risk patients, eg

·        Liver enzyme inducing drugs (carbamazepine, rifampicin, phenytoin, St John’s wart)

·        History of regular excess alcohol/existing liver damage

·        Low Glutathione levels eg malnourished, cahexic, HIV (↓hepatic glutathione).

è Treat more aggressively at lower levels.

 

Dose

-        1 tablet = 500mg

-        Normal dose 4g/day – ie 2 tablets qds

-        3x ↑ dose can be fatal!

·        i.e. >12g/day can be fatal

·        or >150mg/kg (if obese use max value of 110kg instead of actual body weight)

 

Clinical Features:

 

-        Asymptomatic in early stage

-        Vomiting, RUQ pain, stupor, coma.

-        Later fulminant hepatic failure (jaundice and encephalopathy)

 

Investigations:

 

-        Measure Blood paracetamol level AT 4 HOURS post-ingestion

-        FBC, U+E, LFTs, Glucose, Coag, ABGs

-        Cannot discharge until INR is normal (continue treatment)

 

Management:

 

-        Depends on level and time since ingestion

 

If < 1 hour

-        à activated charcoal

·        Charcoal has a large surface area for binding toxins and so decreases absorption from GIT. Also increases vomiting.

 

Then await paracetamol result (measure at 4 hours). If above treatment line give

 

-        N-acetylcysteine (NAC; Parvolex)

 

·        Normally glutathione binds paracetemol. If OD, glutathionine stores are exhausted > pararcetamol toxic to liver

·        Acetylcysteine works like glutathione, binding toxic metabolites of paracetamol

·        Give as infusion over 20 hours

-        s/e: rash (common)

·        Do not stop infusion unless anaphylactic shock, vomiting and wheeze. If occurs, seek specialist help then try methionine.

 

-        If <8 hours

·        Request urgent plasma paracetamol analysis

·        If > treatment line à acetylcysteine infusion (300mg/kg over 20 hours)

 

-        If > 8 hours

·        N-acetylcysteine may still be beneficial

·        If suspected dose >150mg/kg do not await paracetamol level. Commence treatment.

 

Note: If staggered overdose then plasma paracetamol levels are not helpful. Consider treatment or seek advice.

 

Criteria for referral to Transplant Centre

•        Encephalopathy

•        Acidosis

•        Renal failure

•        Severe derangement of clotting

 

 

Salicylate Overdose

-        Aspirin is a weak acid (present in many OTC medications)

-        Inhibits aerobic respiration à production of lactate and heat

 

-        Unlike in paracetamol OD, early symptoms are common

·        vomiting, dehydration, tinnitus, hyperventilation, vertigo, sweating

-        Can progress to coma, seizures, heart block, hyperthermia and death.

 

-        Initially the patient develops a respiratory alkalosis due to direct stimulation of central respiratory centre in the medulla. However as lactate levels rise the patient develops a metabolic acidosis.

è Monitor ABGs

 

Management

 

-        Activated charcoal if <1 hour

-        Measure salicylate level (repeat after 2 hours as may be continuing absorption). Also measure paracetamol level.

-        Resuscitate

·        Fluids, cardiac monitoring

·        Monitor urine output, blood glucose and ABGs

-        If high levels, consider alkalinisation of the urine (bicarbonate and potassium infusion given with 1.5litre dextrose) under senior guidance. Increases urinary excretion of weak acids following OD.

-        May need critical care and renal dialysis.

 

 

Tricyclic Overdose

 

Symptoms

è Largely due anticholinergic effect

 

-        Sinus tachycardia, dry mouth, dilated pupils, urinary retentions

-        ECG – prolongation of QRS = risk of VT. Other abnormalities (eg QT prolongation, heart block) have been reported.

-        Ataxia, nsytagmus, ↓GCS, increased tone, coma, seizure.

-        Hypotension and hypothermia

 

Management

 

-        O2

-        Activated charcoal (oral or by NG tube) – if <1hr and airway protected.

-        If QRS prolongation – treat acidosis. Give infusion of Sodium bicarbonate (in ICU with senior supervision)

-        Lorazepam if convulsions

 

Lithium Overdose

-        Lithium is used for BPAD and as an adjunct in depression

-        Overdose can be acute or accumulated (esp if slow-release preparation)

-        May be triggered by ↓renal function, dehydration (intercurrent illness), drug interaction (NSAIDs or diuretics)

-        Lithium is primarily neurotoxic in overdose

-        It is normally excreted by the kidneys

 

Symptoms

 

-        Onset can be delayed (take up to 24 hours)

 

-        Mild – nausea, diarrhoea, blurred vision, polyuria, polydipsia, fine resting tremor, muscle weakness, metallic taste

 

-        Moderate – confusion, blackouts, fasciculations, mycolconic jerks, choreaoathetoid movements, urinary and faecal incontinence, hypernatraemia

 

-        Severe – coma, convulsions, cerebellar signs (ataxia, tremour), cardiac dysrhythmias (heart block), hypotension, renal failure.

 

Management

 

-        ABCs

-        Gastric lavage

·        Note: Activated charcoal does NOT work in a lithium overdose

-        Haemodialysis!!!!!

·        Perform in all patients with neuro signs

·        Effective at reducing serum lithium levels
